Where the sonnet came from

The sonnet was not Shakespeare's invention. It began in 13th-century Sicily, was perfected by the Italian poet Petrarch in his 14th-century Canzoniere (a sequence of 366 poems addressed to a woman named Laura), and was carried to England by the diplomat and poet Sir Thomas Wyatt in the 1530s. By the 1590s, when Shakespeare wrote his sequence of 154 sonnets, writing sonnets was a competitive, prestigious literary game in England: poets vied to sound surprising while obeying the same tight rules everyone knew. The older Petrarchan (Italian) form splits its fourteen lines into an eight-line octave that poses a problem and a six-line sestet that answers it. Shakespeare worked in a different architecture, the one we now call the Shakespearean (English) sonnet: three quatrains, each a four-line unit with alternating rhymes, followed by a closing two-line couplet. The full rhyme scheme is ABAB CDCD EFEF GG.

A sonnet is not simply a short poem. It is a machine for making an argument, and its shape decides how the argument can unfold. The three quatrains approach a subject from three angles, building pressure stanza by stanza; the couplet then delivers the verdict in two compressed, memorable lines. The hinge between building and delivering is the volta, an Italian word meaning "turn": the moment where the poem pivots from developing its situation to resolving it. In the Petrarchan form the volta usually falls after line 8; in the Shakespearean form it most often arrives at line 9, or at the couplet itself.

The heartbeat: iambic pentameter

Every line of a sonnet runs on the same rhythm. An iamb is one unstressed syllable followed by one stressed syllable: da-DUM. Pentameter means five of them per line: da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM, ten syllables in all. Say the first line of Sonnet 18 aloud and feel where the weight falls: "Shall I com-PARE thee TO a SUM-mer's DAY?" That steady beat is close to the natural rhythm of spoken English, which is why a sonnet can sound like a person thinking out loud even while it obeys strict rules. You will write in this rhythm yourself this Friday.

📚 PART 2 · THE WHOLE MACHINE, LABELED: SONNET 18

Here is Sonnet 18 in full, with the architecture marked. Read it aloud once straight through before you study the labels. Then read it again, watching the rhyme letters in gold and the turn at line 9.

SONNET 18 · WILLIAM SHAKESPEARE · THE ARCHITECTURE, ANNOTATED
QUATRAIN 1 · LINES 1–4 · PROPOSES THE COMPARISON
1Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?A
2Thou art more lovely and more temperate:B
3Rough winds do shake the darling buds of May,A
4And summer's lease hath all too short a date:B
QUATRAIN 2 · LINES 5–8 · TESTS THE COMPARISON
5Sometime too hot the eye of heaven shines,C
6And often is his gold complexion dimm'd;D
7And every fair from fair sometime declines,C
8By chance, or nature's changing course, untrimm'd;D
↻ THE VOLTA · THE TURN ARRIVES WITH "BUT"
QUATRAIN 3 · LINES 9–12 · THE COUNTER-CLAIM
9But thy eternal summer shall not fade,E
10Nor lose possession of that fair thou owest;F
11Nor shall Death brag thou wander'st in his shade,E
12When in eternal lines to time thou growest;F
THE COUPLET · LINES 13–14 · THE VERDICT, IN TWO GILDED LINES
13So long as men can breathe, or eyes can see,G
14So long lives this, and this gives life to thee.G
Indigo brackets mark the three quatrains; the gold letters at the right trace the rhyme scheme ABAB CDCD EFEF GG; the gilded box is the couplet. This poem is in the public domain. The text above follows the widely used modernized text; you can compare the original 1609 spelling at Wikisource: Shakespeare's Sonnets.

📚 WORD STUDY
sonnet — a fourteen-line poem in iambic pentameter with a fixed rhyme scheme.  ·  iambic pentameter — a line of five iambs: da-DUM five times, ten syllables.  ·  quatrain — a four-line unit that develops one stage of the argument.  ·  couplet — the closing pair of rhyming lines that delivers the verdict.  ·  volta — the turn: the moment the poem's argument changes direction.
Word origins: sonnet comes from the Italian sonetto, "little song," from suono, "sound." A sonnet is small enough to sing and strict enough to memorize. Volta is the Italian word for "turn"; you still hear it in revolve and revolt. Copia (in your Organizer): take the sentence "The poem changes direction at line 9," and rewrite it twice: once using volta accurately, and once so the sentence describes a turn in a song, film, or game instead of a poem.
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND FORM
An AI tool can recite the rules of the sonnet form and even count syllables in a line for you. It cannot hear the turn at line 9 land in your own voice when you read the poem aloud. That hearing is the skill this module trains, and it is built by reading, not by asking.
