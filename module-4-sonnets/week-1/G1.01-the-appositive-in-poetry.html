GRAMMAR 1.01
The Appositive in Poetry
Lesson
🎯 TODAY'S FOCUS
How can you name a thing and describe it at the same time, without adding a single verb?
You will learn to identify appositives, tell the essential kind from the removable kind, punctuate each correctly, and watch Shakespeare use the renaming move to pack a metaphor into three words.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 DO THIS IN YOUR ORGANIZER FIRST · NO WRONG ANSWER
Rename someone you know in one phrase, without using their name. For example: my co-conspirator in every bad idea, or the person who always answers at midnight. Write your phrase down. What kind of information did your phrase add?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

An appositive is a noun or noun phrase placed directly beside another noun to rename or explain it: a second label that follows the first with no connecting verb. You just wrote one in the warm-up. Appositives come in two kinds. A restrictive appositive is essential: it narrows down which thing you mean, so it takes no commas. The poet Shakespeare wrote 154 sonnets needs the name to tell us which poet. A non-restrictive appositive adds information the sentence could live without, so it is set off with commas, like handles for lifting it out: Shakespeare, the most celebrated playwright in the English language, wrote 154 sonnets. Poets prize the appositive because it lets a line name a thing and transform it in the same breath. In Sonnet 18, "the eye of heaven" renames the sun: a full metaphor delivered in a three-word noun phrase, with no clause and no extra verb to slow the line down.

ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
If the sentence still makes sense with the renaming phrase removed → commas around it. If removing it leaves the reader asking "which one?" → no commas.
ON THE BOARD · SPOT THE APPOSITIVE, IN VERSE AND IN PROSE
Verse (Sonnet 18, line 5):  Sometime too hot the eye of heaven shines…
Prose (essential, no commas):  The poet Shakespeare wrote 154 sonnets.
Prose (removable, commas):  Shakespeare, the most celebrated playwright in English, wrote 154 sonnets.
Prose (removable, commas):  The sun, that restless eye of heaven, never stays anywhere long.
The gold underline marks the renaming phrase. In the verse line the appositive is fused into the metaphor itself: heaven has an eye, and that eye is the sun. In prose, the commas appear only when the phrase could be lifted out.
✎ YOUR TURN · CHOOSE THE BEST ANSWER
1. Which sentence is punctuated correctly?
2. Which part is the appositive? — My neighbor, a retired architect, builds birdhouses every summer.
➡️ NOW WRITE YOUR OWN (IN YOUR ORGANIZER)

Write three sentences with non-restrictive appositives, each set off with commas. Draw your material from the world of Sonnet 18: use words like summer, beauty, time, shade, gold, or eyes in at least one. Each appositive must add characterization, not just repeat a name. Then read each sentence aloud without the appositive: if it still makes sense, your commas are in the right place. Keep these sentences; renaming phrases like this belong in the sonnet you draft on Friday.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

An appositive names a thing twice: once plainly, once with meaning. Commas when it is removable, none when it is essential. In a ten-syllable line where every word costs, the appositive is how a poet buys a whole metaphor for the price of a noun phrase.
