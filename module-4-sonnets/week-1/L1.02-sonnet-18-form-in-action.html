📚 PART 1 · THE QUESTION THE POEM ASKS
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Is it possible to preserve something beautiful forever? What would have to be true for that to work?

A compliment that starts as a question

Sonnet 18 opens with what sounds like praise but is actually a proposal under review: "Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?" Shakespeare raises the comparison and then immediately begins testing it. The first quatrain proposes the analogy and registers the first doubts; the second quatrain methodically lists summer's failures. The sun (called "the eye of heaven") is sometimes too hot; its "gold complexion" is often dimmed by clouds; and "every fair from fair sometime declines," meaning every beautiful thing eventually loses its beauty, by accident or simply by nature running its course. This is argument by elimination: Shakespeare makes the standard of comparison look inadequate before he replaces it. Summer cannot hold the job of describing the beloved, because summer itself does not last.

Then the volta. Line 9 opens with the single pivot word "But," and the counter-claim arrives fully formed: "But thy eternal summer shall not fade." The beloved is declared exempt from everything the previous eight lines proved about the world. Exempt how? The third quatrain answers: the beloved grows "in eternal lines," the lines of this poem. The couplet seals it with two conditions and a verdict: "So long as men can breathe, or eyes can see, / So long lives this, and this gives life to thee." As long as people live and read, the poem lives, and the poem keeps the beloved alive. The answer to time is not nature and not luck. It is the poem itself.

📚 PART 2 · THE ARGUMENT, MAPPED ONTO THE POEM

Here is the full poem again, this time annotated for its argument rather than its rhyme. Read each section, then its margin note. The form and the argument are the same map: each quatrain is one stage of the case, and the couplet is the ruling.

SONNET 18 · THE ARGUMENT, STAGE BY STAGE
QUATRAIN 1 · LINES 1–4
Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?
Thou art more lovely and more temperate:
Rough winds do shake the darling buds of May,
And summer's lease hath all too short a date:
STAGE 1 · PROPOSE
The comparison is raised as a question, and doubts appear at once: you are lovelier and steadier than summer, which is windy and brief.
QUATRAIN 2 · LINES 5–8
Sometime too hot the eye of heaven shines,
And often is his gold complexion dimm'd;
And every fair from fair sometime declines,
By chance, or nature's changing course, untrimm'd;
STAGE 2 · DISMANTLE
Summer's failures, cataloged: too hot, too dim, and doomed like every fair thing. The comparison collapses under evidence.
QUATRAIN 3 · LINES 9–12 · VOLTA AT "BUT"
But thy eternal summer shall not fade,
Nor lose possession of that fair thou owest;
Nor shall Death brag thou wander'st in his shade,
When in eternal lines to time thou growest;
STAGE 3 · COUNTER-CLAIM
The turn. The beloved is declared exempt: not fading, not losing beauty, not claimed by Death, because the beloved lives in "eternal lines."
COUPLET · LINES 13–14
So long as men can breathe, or eyes can see,
So long lives this, and this gives life to thee.
STAGE 4 · VERDICT
The ruling: as long as people breathe and read, "this" (the poem) lives, and the poem keeps the beloved alive. Writing is an act of preservation.
Public-domain text (modernized). Original 1609 spelling: Wikisource: Shakespeare's Sonnets.

Two devices that carry the argument

First, apostrophe: Shakespeare addresses the beloved directly as "thee" throughout, even though the beloved is not present. Direct address makes the argument feel like an urgent personal declaration rather than an essay about beauty. Second, extended metaphor: the comparison to summer is not a one-line flourish; it is sustained across eight full lines, long enough for Shakespeare to build and then demolish a complete case. Notice also the word temperate in line 2: it means moderate and steady, free of extremes, and it quietly sets up everything quatrain 2 says about summer's wild swings of heat and dimness. One more thing worth knowing: the beloved is never named or described. Scholars have debated for centuries who the poem addresses. The poem preserves a person about whom it tells us almost nothing, which makes its claim about preservation stranger and bolder than it first appears.

📚 WORD STUDY
apostrophe — addressing an absent person or an abstraction directly, as if present.  ·  extended metaphor — a comparison sustained across many lines rather than dropped after one.  ·  temperate — moderate and steady; free of extremes.
Word origins: apostrophe comes from Greek apostrephein, "to turn away": the speaker turns from the audience to address someone who is not there. Copia (in your Organizer): take the sentence "The writer compares life to a journey," and rewrite it twice: once so the comparison is clearly an extended metaphor, and once so the sentence uses apostrophe (address the journey directly).

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

Sonnet 18 wins its argument by using its shape: two quatrains of evidence against summer, a volta that flips the case, and a couplet that names the true preserver. When a sonnet's form and argument move together like this, the form is not decoration. It is the argument.
