🎯 TODAY'S FOCUS
What punctuation mark says "stay with me; this next thought belongs to the last one"?
You will learn when a semicolon can join two sentences, how it differs from a comma and a period, and why Shakespeare uses one to keep the pressure building in the middle of Sonnet 18.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 DO THIS IN YOUR ORGANIZER FIRST · NO WRONG ANSWER
Write two short sentences that belong together, like: "The sky turned gray before noon. The picnic went on anyway." Read them aloud. What would you use to make them one sentence, without "and" or "but"?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

On the scale of pause, the semicolon sits between the comma and the period. A comma joins related pieces inside one sentence; a period ends a thought completely. The semicolon does what neither can: it joins two independent clauses, each able to stand alone as a sentence, while insisting they belong together. It can signal contrast (She arrived early; the room was already full), consequence (He ignored the warnings; the roof came off by midnight), or elaboration (The sonnet is not merely a pretty form; it is a machine for making an argument). What you never get with a semicolon is a joining word spelling the relationship out. The semicolon trusts the reader to see the connection. The test is simple: if you could put a period there and be left with two complete sentences, a semicolon is legal. If either side cannot stand alone, it is not.

THE THREE-QUESTION TEST
1. Are both sides complete sentences?  2. Are they so closely related that a period would feel abrupt?  3. Is the connection obvious without a joining word?  Three yeses → the semicolon earns its place.
ON THE BOARD · THE SEMICOLON AT WORK
Shakespeare (Sonnet 18, lines 5–6):
Sometime too hot the eye of heaven shines,
And often is his gold complexion dimm'd;
Contrast:  Summer is beautiful; it never lasts.
Elaboration:  The couplet is short; it carries the whole verdict.
The gold semicolon holds two complete statements together. In lines 5–6 it closes one pair of complaints about the sun and keeps the reader inside the growing list of summer's failures; a period would let the pressure escape.
✎ YOUR TURN · DECIDE IN YOUR ORGANIZER FIRST, THEN CLICK EACH CARD TO CHECK
CARD 1 · SEMICOLON: YES OR NO?
"The beloved will not fade" + "the poem preserves what time would take."
Tap to reveal
ANSWER
Yes. Both sides are complete sentences, tightly related, and the connection (cause) is obvious: The beloved will not fade; the poem preserves what time would take. A comma alone here would be a comma splice.
CARD 2 · SEMICOLON: YES OR NO?
"She studied the poem for an hour" + "running out of ink halfway through her notes."
Tap to reveal
ANSWER
No. The second part is not a complete sentence; it is a phrase with no subject and no main verb. Attach it with a comma instead: She studied the poem for an hour, running out of ink halfway through her notes.
➡️ NOW WRITE YOUR OWN (IN YOUR ORGANIZER)

Rewrite the couplet of Sonnet 18 ("So long as men can breathe, or eyes can see, / So long lives this, and this gives life to thee") as one prose sentence containing a correctly used semicolon. Make explicit what "this" refers to. Then write one original sentence about your own sonnet subject that uses a semicolon to set up a contrast. Run both through the three-question test before you call them done.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

The period says I am done. The semicolon says stay with me; this matters to what I just said. Both sides must be complete sentences. Use it when the connection is strong enough to feel, and clear enough that no joining word is needed.
