🎯 TODAY'S FOCUS
Why do some lists march like a drumbeat while others trip over their own feet?
You will learn what parallel structure is, why it is an argument-making tool and not just a polish, how Sonnet 116 uses it, and how to build (and deliberately break) it in your own sonnet lines.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 READ BOTH ALOUD · NO WRONG ANSWER
A. I want to read more poems, traveling somewhere new, and better grades.   |   B. I want to read more poems, travel somewhere new, and earn better grades.
Which version marches evenly, and which one stumbles?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

Parallel structure means that items in a series or a comparison take the same grammatical form. Whatever shape the first item has (an infinitive, a noun phrase, a past-tense verb), the rest must match. But parallelism is more than tidiness. When a writer gives two or three ideas the same grammatical shape, the shape itself makes a claim: these things belong together and deserve equal weight. That is why the great public sentences lean on it: "life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness" gains its force from three noun phrases marching as one. And when a writer breaks an established parallel on purpose, the break is felt like a missed stair: in the sonnets, a snapped pattern is one of the signals that the volta has arrived.

ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
Whatever form the first item in your list takes, every item after it must match. Check lists and comparisons in revision; the ear catches a broken parallel faster than the eye.
ON THE BOARD · PARALLELISM DOING ARGUMENTATIVE WORK IN SONNET 116
Lines 2–4 · two parallel relative clauses, two names for the same failure:
Love is not love
Which alters when it alteration finds,
Or bends with the remover to remove:
Lines 11–12 · two parallel predicates, refusal and endurance as one motion:
Love alters not with his brief hours and weeks,
But bears it out even to the edge of doom.
The gold underlines mark the matched verb shapes. Because "alters" and "bends" share one form, the poem treats altering and bending as the same capitulation. Because "alters not" and "bears it out" share one form, refusing to change and enduring to the end read as two sides of one coin. The grammar is making the argument.
✎ YOUR TURN · DECIDE IN YOUR NOTEBOOK FIRST (PARALLEL OR NOT? IF NOT, WHICH ITEM BREAKS?), THEN TAP EACH CARD TO CHECK
CARD 1 · PARALLEL OR NOT?
"She loved the sonnet for its music, its logic, and because it rhymed."
Tap to reveal
ANSWER
Not parallel. The list runs noun phrase, noun phrase, then a whole clause ("because it rhymed"). Fix the third item to match the first two: "for its music, its logic, and its rhyme."
CARD 2 · PARALLEL OR NOT?
"Time cuts the rose, dims the eye, and stills the song."
Tap to reveal
ANSWER
Parallel. Three present-tense verb phrases of the same shape: cuts, dims, stills. The matched shapes imply the three losses belong to one force, which is exactly the claim.
CARD 3 · PARALLEL OR NOT?
"To write in meter, to rhyme with care, and reading your lines aloud."
Tap to reveal
ANSWER
Not parallel. Two infinitives ("to write," "to rhyme") and then an -ing phrase ("reading"). Match the first shape: "to write in meter, to rhyme with care, and to read your lines aloud."
➡️ NOW WRITE YOUR OWN (IN YOUR ORGANIZER)

Write two lines for your sonnet draft about your subject: one line (or pair of lines) built on a clean parallel of two or three verb phrases, like "alters not… but bears it out." Then write a second version where you deliberately break the parallel on the last item, and add one sentence: what does the break make the reader feel? Keep whichever version serves your argument.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

Matching shapes make a claim of equal weight; a broken shape makes the reader feel a turn. Use the first inside your quatrains, and save the second for the moment your sonnet changes direction.
