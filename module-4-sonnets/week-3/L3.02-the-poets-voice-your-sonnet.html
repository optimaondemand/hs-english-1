📚 PART 1 · FROM IMITATION TO VOICE
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
You are borrowing Shakespeare's form: fourteen lines, his meter, his rhyme scheme. What will make the finished poem yours and not his?

First, your compass: one plain sentence

Open your draft and reread your first quatrain. Then, in your Organizer, finish this sentence in plain prose: "My sonnet argues that…" No meter, no rhyme, no decoration. If you cannot say it plainly, the poem cannot say it beautifully. This sentence is your compass for everything that follows: every line you add either serves it or gets cut. You will also say this sentence aloud at the end of your recorded performance this week, so make it one you can stand behind.

The three traps

Nearly every first sonnet falls into at least one of three traps. Trap 1: the meter fights the meaning. A word gets chosen because it fits the beat, not because it is the right word, and the line sounds metered but hollow. The fix is almost never to abandon the meter; it is to find the right word first, then rearrange the sentence so the natural stress lands where the beat wants it. Trap 2: the unearned volta. A pivot word appears at line 9, but nothing actually changes; "Yet still I feel the same" is a flag with no turn behind it. The fix: something the speaker knows or sees after the pivot must be new. Trap 3: the couplet that summarizes. The last two lines restate what the quatrains already said, wasting the two most powerful lines in the poem. The fix: make the couplet a verdict, a claim that only makes sense after the argument. Remember what Sonnet 116 does with its last two lines: it does not summarize; it bets.

ON THE BOARD · THREE DRAFT TRAPS AND THEIR FIXES
TRAP 1 · METER FIGHTS MEANING
Trapped:  My grandmother's visage shows her years of toil
Fixed:      My grandmother's worn hands still knead the dough
"Visage" was chosen for the syllable count, not the meaning. The fix keeps the beat and returns to a word the writer would actually say.
TRAP 2 · THE UNEARNED VOLTA
Trapped:  Yet still I feel the sorrow of the rain
Fixed:      Yet rain is how the buried seed gets found
Both lines fly the pivot flag. Only the second one turns: the speaker now sees something in the rain that lines 1 through 8 could not see.
TRAP 3 · THE COUPLET THAT SUMMARIZES
Trapped:  So love is strong, and time will pass away, / And that is what I wanted here to say.
Fixed:      Then let time swing its blade at what it can: / it never yet has harvested a vow.
The trapped couplet repeats the argument. The fixed couplet delivers a verdict and risks something, the way "If this be error…" does.
📚 PART 2 · THE READ-ALOUD TEST AND THE CRAFT STATEMENT

The one diagnostic that catches all three traps

Read your draft aloud. Not once at the end: throughout. The ear catches what the eye misses; a forced stress, a desperate rhyme, and a line that does not sound like you all announce themselves the moment you speak them. Read straight through without stopping, mark every line where you stumbled, then read a second time at half speed and listen hard at your volta and your couplet. The lines you marked are your revision list. If you stumble reading it, your reader will stumble too.

The craft statement: the builder's note beside the building

Your final project includes a short paragraph called a craft statement. It answers three questions in about five to eight sentences: What does your sonnet argue? Which two techniques did you use on purpose, and why (name them plainly: a metaphor, anaphora, parallel structure, a deliberate broken parallel, argument by negation)? Where does your volta fall, and what changes there? A craft statement is not an apology and not a plot summary. It is the builder's note beside the building: here is what I made, and here is why the beams are where they are. Here is what one might look like for a poem you know:

A MODEL CRAFT STATEMENT (FOR SONNET 116, AS ITS AUTHOR MIGHT WRITE ONE)
"My sonnet argues that love is defined by what it refuses to do: it does not change when circumstances change. I used argument by negation in the first quatrain, ruling out false love before naming true love, so that the positive image would land with more force. I also used an extended navigational metaphor, the fixed star and the wandering ship, to make constancy something the reader can picture. My volta comes early, at line 5, on 'O, no!': the poem shifts there from legal negation to direct assertion, and the early turn is deliberate, because a poem about constancy should plant its flag as soon as possible."

⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND YOUR SONNET
An AI tool can produce a metrically clean fourteen-line poem on request. It cannot choose your subject, decide where your volta falls, or explain why you broke a parallel on purpose. Those decisions are the assignment, and your craft statement is where they show. Work that is not yours cannot be defended in a craft statement, and it reads that way.
