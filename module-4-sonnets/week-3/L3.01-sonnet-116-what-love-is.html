Sonnet 116: What Love Is
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
How do you define something as slippery as love? Shakespeare's answer: start by ruling out everything it is not.
Sonnet 116 is the most argued, most quoted poem in the whole sequence. It is read at weddings today, but it is not a soft poem: it is a definition built like a legal case, and it ends with a bet. Today you trace that argument through all three movements, meet its two great metaphors, and weigh what the final couplet wagers.

I will be able to trace how Sonnet 116 builds its definition of love through negation: by naming what love does not do.
2

3
I will be able to analyze what the closing couplet wagers, and why it is the boldest ending in the four sonnets we have read.

📚 PART 1 · A DEFINITION WITH THE FORCE OF AN OATH
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Think of a promise you have seen kept when keeping it was hard: in a book, a film, or your own life. What made the keeping harder than the making?

A wedding word in line 2

Sonnet 116 sits late in the sequence, after poems in which the speaker has accused the beloved of changing. So when it opens, it opens with urgency, and it borrows its first big word from the marriage service. In Shakespeare's England, the officiant asked whether anyone knew of an impediment: a legal barrier, a reason the marriage could not proceed. Shakespeare answers that question before anyone can ask it: Let me not to the marriage of true minds / Admit impediments. The register is legal, almost sworn testimony. This is not a poem that describes love. It is a poem that argues, on the record, what love is.

The strategy: define by negation

Notice what the poem refuses to do for four whole lines: it never says what love is. It says what love is not. Love is not love "which alters when it alteration finds, / Or bends with the remover to remove." That is a deliberate strategy called argument by negation: build the definition by ruling things out. Positive claims are easy to attack ("love is eternal": prove it). Negations are precise: they name the exact pressures love refuses to break under. Only after the ground is cleared does the positive image arrive, and it arrives with a shout.

SONNET 116 · WILLIAM SHAKESPEARE (1609 QUARTO, MODERNIZED SPELLING · PUBLIC DOMAIN)
QUATRAIN 1 · WHAT LOVE IS NOT
Let me not to the marriage of true minds
Admit impediments. Love is not love
Which alters when it alteration finds,
Or bends with the remover to remove:
QUATRAIN 2 · THE PIVOT AND THE STAR — "O, no!" TURNS THE POEM AT LINE 5, EARLY
O, no! it is an ever-fixed mark,
That looks on tempests, and is never shaken;
It is the star to every wand'ring bark,
Whose worth's unknown, although his height be taken.
QUATRAIN 3 · LOVE VERSUS TIME
Love's not Time's fool, though rosy lips and cheeks
Within his bending sickle's compass come;
Love alters not with his brief hours and weeks,
But bears it out even to the edge of doom.
COUPLET · THE WAGER
If this be error, and upon me prov'd,
I never writ, nor no man ever lov'd.
The gold underlines mark the negations: every place the poem defines love by what it is not or does not do. The indigo badge marks the pivot. Count the negations before line 9. Then notice where the turn falls: not at line 9, where the other sonnets put it, but at line 5.
📚 PART 2 · THE STAR, THE SICKLE, AND THE BET

The fixed mark: love as the star you steer by

When the positive image finally comes, it comes from navigation. The "ever-fixed mark" is the pole star, the one point in the night sky that does not appear to move. Sailors on a "wand'ring bark" (a wandering ship) could measure its height above the horizon and know where they were, even in a storm, even with every landmark gone. So the claim is structural, not sentimental: love is not a feeling that responds to the weather. It is the fixed point by which you navigate when the weather is at its worst. The star "looks on tempests, and is never shaken." Its "worth's unknown": you can measure its height, but you cannot price what it is worth to the lost.

The sickle: what time can and cannot reach

The third quatrain names the enemy directly: "Love's not Time's fool." Time appears with a "bending sickle," the curved blade that cuts grain at harvest, an image every reader in 1609 knew as the reaper Death. Within the sickle's compass (its reach, the sweep of its swing) fall "rosy lips and cheeks": physical beauty gets cut down. But love itself stands outside the swing. It "bears it out even to the edge of doom," and doom here is not disaster but Judgment Day, the end of time itself. Love does not merely outlast a season. The claim is that it holds to the last moment there is.

The couplet: not a summary, a wager

Most couplets conclude. This one bets. "If this be error, and upon me prov'd, / I never writ, nor no man ever lov'd." Follow the logic: if this definition of love is wrong, then Shakespeare has never written a word, and no human being has ever loved. Both of those are plainly false; you are holding his writing right now. So, the couplet implies, the definition stands. He stakes his identity as a poet and the reality of all love in history on the argument he just made. Keep this move in mind this week: your own sonnet's couplet is due, and the difference between a couplet that summarizes and a couplet that risks something is the difference Lesson 3.02 will teach.

📚 WORD STUDY
impediment — an obstacle or legal barrier; the wedding-service word for a reason a marriage cannot proceed.  ·  tempest — a violent storm.  ·  compass — reach, range; the sweep of a blade's swing (not the pointing instrument here).  ·  doom — judgment; "the edge of doom" is Judgment Day, the end of time.  ·  alter — to change, to become other than it was.
Word origins: impediment comes from Latin impedire, "to entangle the feet" (from pes, pedis, foot): an obstacle literally trips you. Tempest comes from Latin tempestas, "storm, season," which grew out of tempus, "time": the storm word carries time hidden inside it, which is exactly the poem's subject. Compass is Latin com- + passus, "step together": to pace around, hence a range or sweep. Doom is Old English dōm, "judgment," still visible in "Doomsday." Alter is Latin alter, "other." Copia (in your Organizer): take the sentence "Nothing could change how she felt," and rewrite it twice: once so the change is blocked by an impediment, and once using alter so the sentence echoes line 3 of the poem.
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND A 400-YEAR-OLD ARGUMENT
An AI tool can paraphrase Sonnet 116 or explain its meter in seconds, and that can be a fair way to check a term. It cannot test the poem's claim against something you have actually seen: a promise kept in a hard year, a constancy you have witnessed. The poem asks for that test, and only you can run it.
