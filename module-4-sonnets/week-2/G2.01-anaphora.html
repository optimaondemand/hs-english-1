GRAMMAR 2.01
Anaphora: Repetition That Builds
Lesson
🎯 TODAY'S FOCUS
Why does starting three sentences the same way feel powerful, when repeating a word anywhere else just feels repetitive?
You will learn to define anaphora precisely, tell it apart from ordinary repetition, find it doing heavy lifting in Sonnet 29, and build a three-clause anaphoric sequence of your own.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 DO THIS IN YOUR ORGANIZER FIRST · NO WRONG ANSWER
Think of a speech, song, or chant where the same phrase keeps returning at the start of each line. Write down the phrase you remembered. What does the repetition do to the feeling of the piece?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

Anaphora is the repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses or lines. The position is the whole definition. A word that shows up several times scattered through a paragraph, in different spots and different jobs, is just a repeated word. Anaphora is structural: the same opening, again and again, on parallel units, so that each new clause inherits the weight of the ones before it. In Sonnet 29, the despair of lines 5–7 is manufactured this way. "Wishing me like to one more rich in hope, / Featured like him, like him with friends possess'd, / Desiring this man's art, and that man's scope." The parallel openings "Wishing" and "Desiring," with "like him, like him" doubling in between, pile longing on longing until the reader carries the weight physically. And the piling has a purpose: when "Yet" finally arrives at line 9, the relief is proportional to the pressure the repetition built. In your own analytical writing, anaphora is a precision tool for building an argument's force. Use it deliberately and sparingly: three parallel openings feel commanding, while six feel mechanical.

ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
Anaphora lives at the front door. If the repeated phrase opens each parallel clause or line → anaphora. If the word merely reappears somewhere inside the sentence → ordinary repetition, no matter how often it occurs.
ON THE BOARD · SPOT THE OPENINGS, IN VERSE AND IN PROSE
Verse (Sonnet 29, lines 5–7):  Wishing me like to one more rich in hope,
       Featured like him, like him with friends possess'd,
       Desiring this man's art, and that man's scope…
Prose (anaphora):  We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields… (Churchill, 1940)
Prose (NOT anaphora):  The song was about love, and love, the singer insisted, was worth any price love asked.
The gold underline marks repetition at the opening of parallel units. In the last line, "love" repeats three times but never opens a parallel clause: that is ordinary repetition, not anaphora. Position, not frequency, makes the device.

➡️ NOW WRITE YOUR OWN (IN YOUR ORGANIZER)

Write a three-clause anaphoric sequence beginning each clause with "This sonnet argues that…" about either Sonnet 29 or Sonnet 130, whichever you know better right now. Each clause must make a different, specific claim: cite a line, name a device, or point to an exact moment. "This sonnet argues that love is good" is too vague to count; "This sonnet argues that one chance thought can outweigh eight lines of envy" earns its place. Read your finished sequence aloud and listen for the build. You will use this exact sentence engine in your analytical writing all module, and a line of deliberate anaphora would not be out of place in the sonnet you draft on Friday.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

Anaphora is repetition with an address: the front of the clause. Used there, and only there, repetition stops being an echo and becomes an engine, stacking weight clause by clause until the sentence, or the sonnet, has something worth turning against.
