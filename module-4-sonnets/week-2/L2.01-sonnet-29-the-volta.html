📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Sonnet 29 spends eight lines at the bottom of a pit and climbs out on a single word. What is that word doing?
Last week you watched Sonnet 18 turn at line 9 on the word "But." Today you meet the most dramatic turn in the whole sequence. Sonnet 29 moves from despair to joy in fourteen lines, and the hinge of that movement, the volta, is the sonnet's most powerful piece of machinery. You will map the two movements, watch how the despair is built so the joy can be earned, and study the lark simile that carries the poem from earth to sky.

📚 PART 1 · EIGHT LINES IN THE PIT

Despair, built by accumulation

Sonnet 29 opens at the bottom. The speaker is "in disgrace with Fortune and men's eyes": out of luck and out of favor, and he knows it. He weeps alone over his "outcast state," sends up "bootless cries" to a heaven he calls deaf (bootless means useless, cries that bring no help), and curses his own fate. That is the first quatrain: shame, isolation, and prayers that bounce off the ceiling. The second quatrain turns the shame into envy, and here the poem starts to pile. The speaker wishes he were "one more rich in hope." He wishes he had another man's looks, "featured like him," and another man's friends, "like him with friends possess'd." He covets "this man's art, and that man's scope": one rival's skill, another rival's range of opportunity. Notice the method. Shakespeare does not state the despair once and move on; he stacks wish on wish, each clause opening the same way, until the weight is almost physical. You will study that stacking move, called anaphora, in this week's first grammar lesson. For now, feel what it does: by line 8 the speaker is so busy wanting other lives that he is "contented least" with the things he actually loves most.

Then line 9. One word, Yet, and the whole poem changes direction. In the middle of "these thoughts," almost despising himself, the speaker happens to think of the beloved: "Haply I think on thee." Haply means by chance. That small word carries a large honesty: the speaker does not argue himself out of despair, and no one talks him out of it. The saving thought simply arrives, unplanned, the way relief actually arrives in life. And when it does, his "state" (his condition, the very word he used for his misery in line 2) rises "like to the lark at break of day arising / From sullen earth" to sing "hymns at heaven's gate." The heaven that was deaf in line 3 has a gate with singing at it by line 12. The couplet then counts the winnings: remembered love brings "such wealth" that the speaker would refuse to trade places with kings. From disgrace to riches, from sullen earth to heaven's gate, in six lines. That is what a volta can do.

📚 PART 2 · THE TWO MOVEMENTS, MAPPED ONTO THE POEM

Here is the full poem, annotated for its emotional argument. Read each section, then its margin note. Sonnet 18 argued in four stages; Sonnet 29 moves in two great movements, down and then up, with the volta as the hinge between them.

SONNET 29 · TWO MOVEMENTS AND A HINGE
QUATRAIN 1 · LINES 1–4 · MOVEMENT 1 BEGINS
When, in disgrace with Fortune and men's eyes,
I all alone beweep my outcast state,
And trouble deaf heaven with my bootless cries,
And look upon myself, and curse my fate,
MOVEMENT 1 · THE PIT
Shame and isolation: out of favor, weeping alone, praying uselessly ("bootless") to a heaven that does not answer, cursing his own fate.
QUATRAIN 2 · LINES 5–8 · THE ENVY CATALOG
Wishing me like to one more rich in hope,
Featured like him, like him with friends possess'd,
Desiring this man's art, and that man's scope,
With what I most enjoy contented least;
MOVEMENT 1 · DEEPER
Envy, stacked clause on clause: another man's hope, looks, friends, skill, and "scope" (range of opportunity). The piling makes the despair heavy enough to need a turn.
QUATRAIN 3 · LINES 9–12 · ⬆️ VOLTA AT "YET"
Yet in these thoughts myself almost despising,
Haply I think on thee, and then my state,
Like to the lark at break of day arising
From sullen earth, sings hymns at heaven's gate;
MOVEMENT 2 · THE RISE
The hinge. One word, "Yet," reverses the poem's direction, and "haply" (by chance) makes the rescue honest: the thought of the beloved arrives unbidden. The lark simile lifts the line from "sullen earth" to "heaven's gate" in a single breath.
COUPLET · LINES 13–14 · THE VERDICT
For thy sweet love remember'd such wealth brings
That then I scorn to change my state with kings.
MOVEMENT 2 · THE PAYOFF
The ruling, in the language of money the octave used for envy: remembered love is "wealth," and the once-outcast speaker now refuses to trade his "state" with kings. The same word, "state," has been transformed.
Public-domain text (modernized). Original 1609 spelling: Wikisource: Shakespeare's Sonnets.

Why this volta feels earned

Two details make Sonnet 29's turn more than a mood swing. First, the word "state" appears three times: the "outcast state" of line 2, the rising "state" of line 10, and the royal "state" of line 14, where the word also means a king's condition and grandeur. The poem measures its own progress by transforming one word. Second, follow the vertical geography. Movement 1 lives low and closed: an outcast on the ground, cries that cannot get through, eyes turned on the self. Movement 2 opens upward: a lark, break of day, hymns, heaven's gate. The lark simile is the engine of that rise, and it works because of where Shakespeare puts the verb. The bird arises "from sullen earth" (sullen means gloomy and heavy), and only at the end of the sentence does the music land: "sings hymns at heaven's gate." Read lines 11 and 12 aloud. The sentence itself withholds its song until the last possible moment, so the reader experiences the lift instead of being told about it. You will study that sentence-building trick, the withheld main clause, in this week's second grammar lesson.

📚 WORD STUDY
volta — the turn: the moment a sonnet's argument reverses, qualifies, or opens into something new.  ·  despair — the complete loss of hope.  ·  scope — range of ability or opportunity; "that man's scope" is the room another man has to act and rise.  ·  sullen — gloomy, heavy, sunk in low spirits; the earth the lark leaves behind.  ·  bootless — useless, achieving nothing; "bootless cries" are prayers that bring no help.  ·  haply — by chance; the saving thought arrives uninvited.
Word origins: volta is Italian for "turn," from Latin volvere, "to roll or turn" (the same root hiding in revolve). Bootless has nothing to do with boots: Old English bot meant "help, remedy, profit," so bootless cries are literally cries without remedy. Despair comes from Latin de + sperare, "away from hoping." And haply grows from hap, "luck," the same root as happen and perhaps. Copia (in your Organizer): take the sentence "The team lost every game that season," and rewrite it three times: once using despair, once using bootless, and once so that something arrives haply and turns the sentence around at its own small volta.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

A volta is only as powerful as the pressure built before it. Sonnet 29 earns its joy by spending eight full lines constructing the despair, wish by wish, so that one small word, "Yet," can release everything at once. When you draft your own sonnet, remember: build the weight first. The turn does the lifting only if there is something heavy to lift.
