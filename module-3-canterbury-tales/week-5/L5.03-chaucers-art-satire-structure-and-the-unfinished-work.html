📚 PART 1 · ONE SATIRIST, MANY BLADES
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
You have now met five tale-tellers. Whose tale surprised you most, given who was telling it?

Satire that never raises its voice

Chaucer's satire almost never announces itself. In the General Prologue the narrator praises everyone, and the praise is the trap: he calls the Prioress's table manners exquisite while her charity goes to lapdogs, and he reports the Pardoner's tricks in the same friendly voice he uses for the Knight's honors. The judgment is left to you. Across the tales the method shifts. The Miller answers the Knight's high romance with a low fabliau, so the two tales satirize each other. The Nun's Priest hides a lesson about pride and flattery inside a barnyard mock epic. The Wife of Bath performs her own defense so vigorously that the defense becomes evidence. And the Pardoner does what no satirist could do better: he satirizes himself, on purpose, and does not care. One author, five different blades, and every one of them cuts by showing rather than telling.

That is the first tool for your essay: when you argue that a pilgrim embodies or betrays a virtue, ask how Chaucer lets you see it. A telling detail in the portrait? A tale that fits its teller too well, or not at all? A voice that reveals more than it means to? The method is your evidence.

📚 PART 2 · THE FRAME, AND THE ROAD THAT NEVER ENDS

What the frame narrative does

A frame narrative is a story that contains other stories: the pilgrimage and the Host's contest are the outer story, and every tale lives inside it. The frame is not just a container. It puts the tales in conversation. The Miller's tale means something different because it interrupts the Knight's. The Wife's tale lands differently because her long prologue comes first. Remove the frame and you have an anthology; keep it and you have an argument among thirty voices about what people are worth.

The unfinished pilgrimage, and the Retraction

Chaucer worked on the Canterbury Tales from roughly 1387 until his death in A.D. 1400, and he never finished it. The plan announced in the General Prologue called for each pilgrim to tell tales going and coming, on the order of a hundred and twenty tales; we have twenty-four, some only fragments. The Host promised a supper for the best tale; no prize is ever awarded. And at the very end of the manuscripts stands one more surprise, the Retraction: a short passage in Chaucer's own voice asking forgiveness for his worldly writings, with the Canterbury Tales named among them. Readers have argued for centuries about whether it is sincere piety, a conventional gesture, a last quiet irony, or all three at once.

Here is why the incompleteness matters to your essay. Because no judge inside the book ever rules on the pilgrims, the ruling falls to the reader. The work hands you the gavel. When you argue that one pilgrim best embodies or most betrays a virtue, you are not decorating a finished monument; you are finishing, for one pilgrim, the judgment the book deliberately left open.

📚 WORD STUDY
frame narrative  a story that contains other stories; the outer story shapes how every inner tale is heard.  ·  synthesis  combining separate ideas or texts into one new claim that none of them makes alone.  ·  retraction  a formal taking-back of earlier words or works.
Word origins: synthesis is Greek, syn ("together") + tithenai ("to place"): a placing-together. Retraction is Latin, re- ("back") + trahere ("to drag or draw"): a drawing-back of what was said, the same root that gives English tractor and extract.
