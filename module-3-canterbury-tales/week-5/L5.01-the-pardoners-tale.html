The Pardoner's Tale: The Self-Confessed Villain

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Can a liar tell the truth? Can a corrupt man preach a sermon that actually works?
The Pardoner is the only pilgrim who tells the others, to their faces, exactly how he cheats them. Then he preaches against the very sin he lives by, and the sermon lands anyway. Today you meet Chaucer's self-confessed villain and the question he is placed last to ask.

3
I will be able to use the words pardoner, indulgence, radix malorum, and hypocrisy accurately in my own analysis.

📚 PART 1 · THE CONFESSION THAT IS NOT A CONFESSION
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Imagine a salesperson opens with this: "I am about to trick you, and here is exactly how." Then they give the pitch anyway. Does knowing the trick make the pitch weaker?

Who is the Pardoner?

A pardoner was a church official licensed to grant an indulgence, a formal remission of the punishment owed for sin, in exchange for a donation meant for hospitals or the poor. The office was open to abuse, and Chaucer's Pardoner has abused it completely. He carries a pillowcase he calls the Virgin's veil and pig bones he sells as the relics of saints, all under the cover of the Pope's seal. What makes him unforgettable is not the fraud; several pilgrims are corrupt. It is that he tells the whole company about it before he begins. His recurring text, the Latin line he preaches from every time, is Radix malorum est Cupiditas: "greed is the root of evil." He uses that warning to pull coins from people who have far less than he does, and he says so plainly.

In his Prologue he admits his only aim in these words:

IN CHAUCER'S MIDDLE ENGLISH
"For myn entente is nat but for to wynne,
And no thyng for correccioun of synne."
Gloss: "For my intent is nothing but to profit, and not at all to correct sin." He preaches against greed only to feed his own.

This is a confession in form but not in spirit. A true confession asks for forgiveness and means to change. The Pardoner asks for nothing and intends to change nothing. He confesses to show off his skill. That is why the word for him is not simply "liar" but hypocrite: he wears the mask of holiness over a face he freely shows us.

📚 PART 2 · THE TALE AS ITS OWN PROOF

Then the Pardoner tells his tale, and it is superb. Three young men, drunk in a tavern, hear that a thief named Death has been killing their neighbors. In a rage they swear an oath to hunt Death down and kill him. On the road an old, poor man tells them where to look: up a crooked path, under a tree. They race there and find not Death but a heap of gold coins. From that moment they forget Death entirely. They plot instead to keep the gold, and their greed turns them against one another. Two murder the third; the third had already poisoned their wine. All three lie dead under the tree. They found Death exactly where the old man said, because they carried it with them in their greed.

The gold is the turning point. The instant they see it (gold underline in your reading), they stop hunting Death and start guarding treasure. That is the moral proving itself inside the story.

Here is the trap Chaucer sets. The tale is a flawless exemplum, a short story told to prove a moral. Its moral is true. Greed does destroy. Yet the man telling it is destroyed by greed and does not care. So the tale forces a hard question: does the truth of a message depend on the character of the messenger? A reader who knows the Pardoner is a fraud still feels the fable's force. That gap between teller and tale is why Chaucer saves him for the end.

📚 WORD STUDY
pardoner (and indulgence)  a church agent who sold indulgences, official pardons remitting the penalty for sin.  ·  radix malorum  Latin for "the root of evils"; the Pardoner's whole text is Radix malorum est Cupiditas, "greed is the root of evil."  ·  hypocrisy  pretending to a virtue or belief one does not hold.
Word origins: indulgence comes from Latin indulgentia, "leniency, a granting of favor." Radix is Latin for "root" and survives in English radish and radical (going to the root). Hypocrisy comes from the Greek hypokrisis, "acting a part on stage," from hypokrites, "a stage actor"; the word remembers that a hypocrite is a performer, which is exactly what the Pardoner is.
Copia (in your Organizer): take the plain sentence "The Pardoner is greedy," and rewrite it twice: once so that the word hypocrisy carries the judgment, and once so that indulgence appears in its exact medieval sense.
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND THE HONEST-SOUNDING VOICE
An AI tool can produce fluent, confident, moral-sounding prose on demand, the way the Pardoner produces a flawless sermon. Fluency is not the same as honesty, and a polished paragraph can still be empty or wrong. When you read AI output, do what this tale trains you to do: judge the claim on its evidence, not on how smooth the voice sounds.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

The Pardoner reveals his soul by revealing his fraud. His tale is true; his life makes a mockery of it. Keep that gap in view: a character can show us the most when what they say and what they are pull in opposite directions.
