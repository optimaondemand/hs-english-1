Revision for Voice and Precision

🎯 TODAY'S FOCUS
Proofreading fixes errors. Revision changes what a sentence is able to do. What is the difference in practice?
Before you finish your Pilgrim's Portrait essay, you will learn four revision moves that sharpen voice and precision: cut the empty opener, trade weak verbs for exact ones, name things specifically, and vary your sentence shapes on purpose.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 READ BOTH ALOUD · NO WRONG ANSWER
A. There are many ways in which the Pardoner is a person who is not honest.
B. The Pardoner sells lies for a living and says so.
Both are grammatically correct. Which one sounds like a writer you would keep reading?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

Voice is the personality your sentences carry: their rhythm, their confidence, the sense that a particular person is thinking on the page. Precision is exactness: the right verb, the specific noun, the claim no bigger and no smaller than your evidence. Weak drafts usually fail at both in the same four places, so revision has four reliable moves:

FOUR REVISION MOVES
1. Cut the empty opener. "There is / There are / It is clear that" delay the real subject. Start with it instead.
2. Trade weak verbs for exact ones. Forms of "to be" plus a vague noun ("is a person who deceives") hide a strong verb ("deceives").
3. Name things specifically. "Bad things happen to the men" vs. "two rioters stab the third over a heap of florins."
4. Vary the sentence shapes. Three same-length sentences in a row lull the reader. Follow a long complex-compound sentence with a short one. It wakes them up.
A REAL REVISION, MARKED UP · DRAFT SENTENCE → REVISED SENTENCE
There are several ways in which it can be seen that the Pardoner is a person who is not honest about things betrays honesty twice over: he is deceptive with swindles people poor villagers with pig bones sold as relics, and, although his sermon against greed is true, he preaches it only for bad reasons to fill his own purse.
Struck-through words are cuts: empty openers, weak "is a person who" phrasing, vague nouns. Gold italic words are the replacements: an exact verb (swindles, betrays), specific nouns (pig bones sold as relics), and a subordinate concession (although his sermon is true) folded in from Grammar 5.02. Same claim, twice the force.

Revision is re-seeing, not polishing. The word comes from Latin revidere, "to see again." When you revise, read each sentence and ask what it is doing for your argument. A sentence that repeats the previous one, hedges a claim you can prove, or buries its verb is not wrong; it is idle. Put it to work or cut it. Chaucer is the model here: his portraits waste nothing, and one exact detail (a gold love-knot brooch, a pillowcase sold as a relic) does the work of a paragraph of adjectives.

📁 M365 SKILL: TRACK CHANGES (WORD)
Revise your essay draft with Review → Track Changes turned on in Word. Your cuts show as strikethroughs and your additions in color, exactly like the notebook example above. When you are satisfied, use Accept All Changes to produce the clean final. Practicing this now means your submitted file shows only the finished prose.
➡️ NOW REVISE YOUR OWN (IN YOUR ESSAY DRAFT)

Open your Quick Write 5 opening paragraph (or your essay draft) and make four marked revisions, one per move: cut one empty opener, replace one weak verb, make one vague noun specific, and adjust one sentence's length for rhythm. Keep Track Changes on so you can see, and show, what changed.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

Revision is seeing your own sentences again and asking each one to earn its place. Exact verbs, specific nouns, varied shapes: that is voice and precision, and it is the last mile between a draft and your finished Pilgrim's Portrait.
