Diction and Register

🎯 TODAY'S FOCUS
"I shall endeavor to attend" and "I'll swing by" mean the same thing. Why do they sound like two different people?
Diction is word choice; register is the level of formality those choices add up to. Chaucer gave the Knight a high register and the Miller a low one, and the difference is a portrait of each man. Today you will learn to place language on the register spectrum and choose your own register on purpose.

📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

Diction is the writer's choice of words: steed or horse or nag; maiden or girl; perish or die or croak. Each choice carries a social temperature. Register is the overall level those choices set, from high (formal, ceremonious, often Latin- or French-derived words) down to low (casual, blunt, often short Anglo-Saxon words). Register is not a scale of good to bad. A high register at a funeral is right; the same register ordering lunch is ridiculous. Skilled writers control register the way musicians control volume.

Chaucer is a master of this control. The Knight's Tale speaks of a duc called Theseus, of chivalrye and solempnytee: French-flavored, ceremonial words for a ceremonial world. The Miller's Tale calls its carpenter a riche gnof (a rich lout) and praises Alisoun by comparing her body to a wesele (a weasel). The vocabulary of each tale is the voice of its teller. When you quote either tale in your essay, name the register you hear; when you write, choose yours deliberately.

ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
Match your register to your purpose and audience, then keep it steady. A register that jumps around by accident sounds like a mistake; a register that shifts on purpose is a tool.
ON THE BOARD · THE REGISTER SPECTRUM, HIGH TO LOW

formal · ceremonial  →  neutral · everyday  →  casual · blunt
HIGH REGISTER
"I shall endeavor to attend."
"We request your presence."
Knight: "a duc that highte Theseus… with muchel glorie and greet solempnitee"
NEUTRAL REGISTER
"I'll try to come."
"Please join us."
Most of what you write for school lives here.
LOW REGISTER
"I'll swing by."
"Get over here."
Miller: "a riche gnof" · a wife "gent and smal" as "any wesele"
Same language, three temperatures. Chaucer writes the Knight at the gold end and the Miller at the blue end, and each teller's register is part of his portrait.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

Every word sits somewhere on the register spectrum, and a paragraph's words vote on its level together. Chaucer used that fact to paint two opposite men in two opposite tales. Use it yourself: pick your register on purpose and hold it steady through your Quick Write and your essay.
