The Miller's Tale: Satire, Fabliaux, and the Vulgar
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
The Knight has just finished his noble tale. Why does a drunk miller shove his way in to answer it with a crude one?
The Host invites the Monk, the next-highest pilgrim, to speak. Instead the Miller, so drunk he can barely sit his horse, insists on going now. His tale is a fabliau: fast, low, and vulgar, and it deliberately parodies the noble story that came before. Today you will see how the tale mocks the Knight's, and what that choice reveals about its teller.

📚 PART 1 · THE INTERRUPTION
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Someone has just told a long, serious, impressive story, and the room applauds. What does it say about the next person if they jump in, uninvited, with a rude joke on the same subject?

"I wol now quyte the Knightes tale"

Chaucer stages the moment carefully. The Host, keeping order by rank, turns to the Monk. But the Miller, "that for-dronken was al pale, / So that unnethe up-on his hors he sat" (so drunk he could barely sit his horse), barges in. He announces: "I can a noble tale for the nones, / With which I wol now quyte the Knightes tale." The key word is quyte: to repay, to answer, to pay back. The Host tries to stop him ("abyd, Robin, my leve brother, / Som bettre man shal telle us first another"), and the Miller refuses. He will speak now or leave. A miller, one of the lowest-ranking pilgrims, demands the floor right after the highest. The interruption is itself a little rebellion against the social order the Knight's Tale just celebrated.

🔖 READ ALONG (PUBLIC DOMAIN, MIDDLE ENGLISH)
Open the Miller's Prologue and Tale in Chaucer's original Middle English: Canterbury Tales, ed. Skeat · The Milleres Tale (Wikisource). Read the Prologue (the interruption), then the tale's opening, where we meet the old carpenter, his young wife Alisoun, the clerk "hende Nicholas," and Absolon the parish clerk. A note before you read: this is a bawdy tale, and Chaucer knew it. He warns squeamish readers to "turne over the leef, and chese another tale." Our focus is on what the vulgarity is doing, not on relishing it.
📚 PART 2 · FOUR WORDS FOR A LOW STORY

Fabliau: the anti-romance

A fabliau (plural fabliaux) is a short, comic, often crude verse tale, usually about ordinary townspeople: tradesmen, clerks, and cunning wives. Where romance runs on honor, fabliaux run on trickery; where romance rewards nobility, fabliaux reward cleverness. The Miller's Tale is the most famous fabliau in English. An old carpenter named John has married a young wife, Alisoun ("Fair was this yonge wyf... As any wesele hir body gent and smal": slim and graceful as a weasel, a townsman's compliment, not a courtly one). Their boarder, the clever clerk hende Nicholas, schemes to win her, while the fussy parish clerk Absolon courts her from the street. The plot is a machine of tricks that ends with everyone except Alisoun humiliated: a far cry from a tournament judged by a duke.

Parody and satire: what the mockery is for

A parody imitates the form of another work in order to mock it. The Miller's Tale is a point-by-point parody of the Knight's: again two young men pursue one woman, again there is a "courtship," again fate seems to hang overhead. But the tower is a carpenter's house, the goddess is a tradesman's wife, and destiny is a fake flood dreamed up to fool a gullible husband. Satire is the broader weapon: comedy aimed at exposing folly or vice. Through the parody, the Miller's satire lands on pretension itself: on solemn ideals that ignore how ordinary people actually behave. The vulgarity is not decoration. It is the argument: your noble world, seen from below, looks like this.

The Knight's Tale gives us…The Miller's Tale answers with…

Emelye, worshiped from a tower windowAlisoun, flirted with across a carpenter's house

A formal tournament settles the rivalryA midnight prank settles it, painfully
Order restored by a wise dukeChaos: a broken arm, a branded backside, a town laughing

Register: the sound of social class

Register is the level of formality in a speaker's language: the difference between "I shall endeavor to attend" and "I'll try to come." The Knight's Tale speaks in a high register: ceremonious, Latinate, formal. The Miller's Tale speaks low: blunt, physical, packed with the words of workshops and barnyards. Chaucer matches each teller's register to his station and his character, which is exactly why the tales reveal their tellers. You will study this tool up close in Grammar 2.04.

📚 WORD STUDY
fabliau (pl. fabliaux): a short, comic, often crude medieval verse tale of ordinary people and trickery.  ·  satire: comedy that exposes folly or vice in order to criticize it.  ·  parody: a mocking imitation of another work's style or form.  ·  register: the level of formality of a speaker's language.
Word origins: fabliau is Old French, a "little fable," from Latin fabula (story), the same root as fable. Satire comes from Latin satura, a "medley" or full dish of mixed things. Parody is from Greek paroidia, a "song sung beside" another, an imitation set next to the original. Register comes from Latin regesta, "things recorded": a list, and later, the range or level something is set at.
Copia (in your Organizer): take the plain sentence "The Miller told a rude story," and rewrite it three times: once using fabliau to name its genre, once using parody to show its target, and once using satire to name what it exposes. Expand each with a detail from the tale.
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND SATIRE
AI tools can summarize a plot and label a genre. They are much less reliable at judging tone: whether a passage is mocking, affectionate, or both at once. Deciding whether the Miller is attacking the Knight, or just refusing to bow to him, requires your own reading of the tone. Do that judging yourself.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

The Miller "quytes" the Knight: he repays a noble romance with a vulgar fabliau built on the same skeleton. The choice reveals the man: irreverent, clever, contemptuous of pretension. Set the two tellers side by side and you have the heart of this week's Quick Write: what a chosen story reveals about the soul that chose it.
