Subordinate Clauses and the Architecture of Argument

🎯 TODAY'S FOCUS
When two ideas share a sentence, how do you show the reader which one is in charge?
A subordinate clause leans on a main clause. That relationship is a tool. In an argument, you put your claim in the main clause and the idea you are only granting or explaining in the subordinate clause. Today you will use that move to write sharper sentences about the tales.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 READ BOTH ALOUD · NO WRONG ANSWER
A. Although the Miller is drunk, his tale exposes real hypocrisy.   |   B. Although his tale exposes real hypocrisy, the Miller is drunk.
In which version is "his tale exposes real hypocrisy" the point the writer wants you to keep?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

Quick review. An independent clause has a subject and a verb and can stand alone: The Miller tells a crude tale. A subordinate (dependent) clause also has a subject and a verb, but it begins with a subordinating conjunction such as although, because, when, while, since, or if, so it cannot stand alone. Joining the two makes a complex sentence.

Here is the new move. In argument, the main clause is the loud one. Readers remember what sits there. So a skilled writer subordinates the concession (the thing you are willing to grant) and keeps the claim in the main clause. "Although his tale is bawdy, the Miller's story tells the truth about greed" concedes the crudeness and still leaves you holding the claim. This lets you sound fair without losing the argument.

ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
Put the concession in the subordinate clause; keep the claim in the main clause. Subordinate clause first → use a comma.
ON THE BOARD · SUBORDINATE THE CONCESSION, KEEP THE CLAIM
Although the Knight's Tale is long, it shows a society restoring order.
The Miller reveals his contempt for rank because he mocks the noble tale that came before.
When a teller chooses his story, he reveals what he values.
The gold underline marks the subordinate clause. In each line the writer's real point sits in the main clause, not in the underlined part.
✎ YOUR TURN · CHOOSE THE BEST ANSWER
1. Which part is the subordinate clause? — Palamon still hopes, although he is locked in the tower.
2. You want to argue that the Miller's tale reveals his character. Which sentence does that best?
➡️ NOW WRITE YOUR OWN (IN YOUR ORGANIZER)

Write two complex sentences about the Knight or the Miller. In each, put a concession in the subordinate clause and your claim in the main clause. Begin one sentence with the subordinate clause (remember the comma) and let the subordinate clause come second in the other. Use a different subordinating conjunction in each.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

Subordination is not just grammar; it is emphasis. Grant the other side in the subordinate clause, keep your claim in the main clause, and your reader hears exactly what you mean to say. You will use this move throughout your module essay.
