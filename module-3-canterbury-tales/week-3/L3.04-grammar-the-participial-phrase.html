LESSON 3.04
The Participial Phrase
Grammar Lesson
🎯 TODAY YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
Spot a participial phrase, name the noun it describes, punctuate it correctly, avoid the dangling modifier, and use one to combine two short sentences about this week's tales.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 READ BOTH ALOUD · NO WRONG ANSWER
A. Chanticleer trusted the fox. He shut both eyes to sing.   |   B. Trusting the fox, Chanticleer shut both eyes to sing.
Both are correct. What did version B do to the two sentences?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

A participle is a verb form used as an adjective. Present participles end in -ing (trusting, crowing, laughing); past participles usually end in -ed or -en, or take an irregular form (deceived, hidden, caught). A participial phrase is the participle plus the words that travel with it, and the whole phrase works as one big adjective describing a noun: in Trusting the fox, Chanticleer shut both eyes, the phrase Trusting the fox describes Chanticleer. Writers love this structure because it lets a sentence do two things at once: the main clause carries the action while the phrase adds the posture, motive, or condition of the actor.

Two habits keep the structure clean. First, when the phrase opens the sentence, follow it with a comma. Second, the phrase must sit beside the noun it describes, or it "dangles" and grabs the wrong noun: Running from the fox, the tale grows louder is wrong, because a tale cannot run. Put the true runner next to the phrase: Running from the fox, Chanticleer set the whole yard shrieking.

ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
Opening participial phrase → comma → the noun it describes.
Charmed by the praise, Chanticleer began to sing. (Chanticleer is the one charmed, so the phrase points at the right noun.)
ON THE BOARD · PARTICIPIAL PHRASES · EACH DESCRIBES A NOUN
Fearing a second flood, the carpenter slept in a tub beneath the roof.
Charmed by the fox’s praise, Chanticleer stretched his neck and shut both eyes.
The fox, opening his mouth to boast, lost his prize in an instant.
Branded by the red-hot iron, Nicholas screamed the one word that ruined them all.
The gold participial phrase sits right beside the noun it describes. Fearing and opening are present participles (-ing); charmed and branded are past participles. Notice the commas: one after an opening phrase, a pair around a phrase in the middle.

✎ MORE PRACTICE (IN YOUR NOTEBOOK FIRST)
In each sentence, find the participial phrase and name the noun it describes. Write your answers before you open the check.
1. Warned by his dream, Chanticleer still flew down into the yard.
2. Pertelote, dismissing the dream as bad digestion, prescribed a laxative.
3. Caught in the fox's jaws, the rooster finally began to think.
▶  Write your answers, then check
1. Warned by his dream → describes Chanticleer (past participle "warned"; comma after the opening phrase).
2. dismissing the dream as bad digestion → describes Pertelote (present participle; set off with commas in the middle).
3. Caught in the fox's jaws → describes the rooster (past participle "caught").
➡️ NOW WRITE YOUR OWN (IN YOUR ORGANIZER)

Take two short sentences about the Miller's Tale or the Nun's Priest's Tale and combine them by turning one into an opening participial phrase (remember the comma). Then write one more sentence with the phrase in the middle. Check each: does the phrase sit right beside the noun it describes, with no dangling modifier? You will want this move in Quick Write 3, where a phrase like "Hidden inside the comedy," can carry your whole argument.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

A participial phrase is a describing phrase built on a verb. It lets one sentence show action and attitude together, but only if it sits beside the noun it describes. Keep the doer next to the phrase and nothing will dangle.
