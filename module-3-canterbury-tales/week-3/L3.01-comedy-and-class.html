Comedy and Class: What the Miller's Tale Gets Away With
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Why does a story about a broken arm and a scream for water make people laugh, and what is Chaucer allowed to say through that laughter that he could not say straight?
Last week you met the Miller's Tale. Today you watch its ending snap shut like a trap, and you learn the name for a tale like this: the fabliau. Then you look at who is telling it, and why comedy lets a drunk miller mock a wealthy carpenter, marriage, and piety all at once, and walk away grinning.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to define a fabliau and name its usual features.
2
I will be able to explain how the tale's ending delivers comic justice, matching each punishment to a fault.
3
I will be able to argue what the choice of a low, comic tale reveals about the Miller and about social class.

📚 PART 1 · WHY WE LAUGH WHEN IT GOES WRONG
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Think of a comedy where a character who has been scheming, bragging, or bullying finally gets exactly what is coming to them. When the payback lands, how does it feel?

A tale built to spring shut

Early in the tale Chaucer plants three separate loose threads, and you almost forget they are loose. The clever student Nicholas has convinced John, a jealous and well-off old carpenter, that a second Noah's flood is coming, so John spends the night hanging from the roof in a tub, waiting to float away. Meanwhile Absolon, a vain parish clerk, is humiliated at the couple's window and swears revenge. Each thread has been set up. Then, in a few fast lines, all three pay off at the same instant. Absolon returns with a red-hot iron and brands Nicholas. Nicholas, in shock, screams a single word: "Water!" The carpenter, jolted awake, hears only that word, believes the flood has finally come, and cuts the rope on his tub. He crashes to the floor and breaks his arm. The whole street pours in, and they do not comfort him. They laugh.

This is the shape of a fabliau: a short, bawdy comic tale about ordinary people, driven by trickery, appetite, and a clever plot that pays everyone back at once. Fabliaux (the plural) do not deal in noble knights and pure love. They deal in landlords, students, tradesmen, lust, money, and revenge, and they end with a punchline of rough poetic justice.

THE COMIC MACHINE · THREE SET-UPS, ONE PUNCHLINE, EACH FAULT REPAID
THREAD 1 · THE CARPENTER
Fault: jealous, controlling, gullible
Payback: hears "Water!", cuts the rope, falls, breaks his arm, is mocked by the town.
THREAD 2 · NICHOLAS
Fault: too clever, too pleased with his own scheme
Payback: branded by the hot iron at the very moment he expects to win.
THREAD 3 · ABSOLON
Fault: vain, squeamish, easily wounded pride
Payback: gets his revenge, but only after a humiliation he can never live down.
One word, "Water!", trips all three threads at once. The plot pays back every character in the exact currency of his own flaw. That is comic justice.
📚 PART 2 · COMEDY AND CLASS: WHAT THE TALE GETS AWAY WITH

Now ask the question that runs through this whole module: what does the choice of tale reveal about the teller? The Knight, the highest-ranked pilgrim, told a lofty romance about noble love and honor. The Miller, drunk and low-born, refuses to wait his turn and insists on going next. His tale is the opposite of the Knight's in every way: instead of princes, a carpenter and a student; instead of pure love, appetite and a trick; instead of a solemn ending, a broken arm and a street full of laughter. In the language of the period, the Miller quites the Knight. To quite is to pay back or answer, and the Miller answers the Knight's high ideals by dragging them down to earth.

This is what comedy gets away with. Under the cover of a joke, the tale mocks a wealthy tradesman, pokes at marriage, and even plays with the sacred story of Noah's flood, and yet it feels like harmless fun. Chaucer knows exactly what he is doing. He steps in as narrator and warns you that the Miller is a churl, a coarse low-born man, and tells you to blame the Miller, not the poet, and to turn the page if you are easily offended. That disclaimer is itself a joke. It lets Chaucer print the whole tale while pretending to disown it. Comedy gives the powerless a licensed way to laugh at the powerful, and it gives the poet a way to say the unsayable and call it entertainment.

📚 WORD STUDY
fabliau — a short, bawdy comic tale of ordinary people and clever tricks.  ·  churl — a coarse, low-born person.  ·  comeuppance — a deserved punishment or downfall.  ·  quite (Middle English) — to pay back or answer.
Copia (in your Organizer): take the sentence "The carpenter got what he deserved," and rewrite it twice: once using comeuppance, and once using quite so it names who is paying whom back.
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND WHAT COUNTS AS FUNNY
An AI tool can list the features of a fabliau or summarize the plot in seconds, which can be a fine way to check a definition. It cannot tell you why a six-hundred-year-old joke still lands, or decide what a modern reader should find fair or cruel about it. Judging what a text is really doing to you as you read stays your work.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

A fabliau hides a sharp argument inside a joke. The Miller's ending is not just slapstick: it pays every character back in the currency of his own flaw, and it lets a low-born teller answer a noble one. Comedy is how Chaucer says the dangerous thing and smiles.
