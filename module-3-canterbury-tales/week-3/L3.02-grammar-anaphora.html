📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 READ BOTH ALOUD · NO WRONG ANSWER
A. The Miller mocks the carpenter, marriage, and pious stories.   |   B. The Miller mocks the carpenter. The Miller mocks marriage. The Miller mocks the pious stories themselves.
Both say the same thing. What does version B do that version A does not?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

Anaphora is the deliberate repetition of the same word or phrase at the beginning of successive sentences, clauses, or lines. The word comes from Greek: ana- (back, again) plus pherein (to carry), so anaphora literally "carries back" the reader to the same starting point before launching forward again. Writers reach for it when they want a passage to feel like a mounting wave: each repetition raises the pressure. Chaucer's narrators love this move. When the Nun's Priest mourns the fox's attack this week, he piles up cries of "O" line after line, addressing destiny, then Venus, then a famous rhetorician, as if a rooster's bad day were the fall of Troy. The repetition is the joke, and the joke works because the device is real: repetition genuinely does make language sound grander.

One caution. Repetition is only a device when it is chosen. If a paragraph repeats "Then" at the start of five sentences because the writer was not paying attention, that is wordiness, not anaphora. The test is simple: does the repetition build toward something, and would the passage lose force if you removed it? If yes, it is anaphora. If it would read better without the repeats, revise.

ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
Anaphora = the same opening, repeated on purpose, building to a final strike. Three repetitions is the classic shape: two to set the pattern, one to pay it off.
ON THE BOARD · ANAPHORA · THE REPEATED OPENING IS UNDERLINED
The pilgrims ride to be healed. The pilgrims ride to be seen. The pilgrims ride, above all, to tell their tales.
One word undoes the carpenter. One word brands Nicholas a fool. One word, "Water!", brings the whole house down.
O cursed day! O treacherous fox! O rooster, too easily charmed!
Each set repeats its gold opening and saves the strongest item for last. The third example imitates the Nun's Priest's mock-grand cries of "O": grand style, barnyard subject.

✎ MORE PRACTICE (IN YOUR NOTEBOOK FIRST)
For each item, write down the repeated opening and what the repetition builds toward. Do both in your notebook before you open the check.
1. Every pilgrim has a portrait. Every portrait has a telling detail. Every telling detail gives the soul away.
2. The fox flatters the rooster's song. The fox flatters the rooster's father. The fox flatters him right off his perch.
▶  Write your answers, then check
1. Repeated opening: "Every…" (every pilgrim, every portrait, every telling detail). It builds a chain from the whole company down to the single detail that "gives the soul away," which is the module's central question.
2. Repeated opening: "The fox flatters…". Each repetition escalates the flattery until the final clause pays it off with the consequence: charmed right off his perch.

This move will be useful in your Quick Write this week, where you argue what comedy is really doing.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

Anaphora is repetition with a destination: the same opening, chosen on purpose, building to a final strike. Chaucer uses it to make a rooster sound like a fallen king. You can use it to make your best point land hardest.
