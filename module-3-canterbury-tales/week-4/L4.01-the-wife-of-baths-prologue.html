📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
When a person argues about her own life, who do we trust: the books, or the woman who has lived it?
Most of Chaucer's pilgrims are described from the outside. The Wife of Bath describes herself, at length, and builds a case. Today you will meet a narrator who is also an arguer: you will see how she pits experience against authority, and you will start weighing whether her rhetoric convinces you or merely dazzles you.

📚 PART 1 · A NARRATOR WHO ARGUES
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Imagine two people telling you how to succeed. One quotes a stack of expert books. The other says, "I have done this five times; let me tell you what actually happens." Whose advice do you lean toward first?

What is rhetoric?

Rhetoric is the art of persuasion: the deliberate choices a speaker makes to move an audience. It is not a bad word. Every lawyer, preacher, and advertisement uses rhetoric. What makes rhetoric worth studying is that it can serve truth or disguise its absence, and the same techniques do both. So the reader's job is double: notice the moves, then decide whether the case underneath them holds. The Wife of Bath gives you a master class in both halves.

Her opening move: experience against authority

The Wife does not ease in. Her very first lines stake a claim, and the claim is about where knowledge comes from. In the medieval world, auctoritee meant the settled wisdom of books, scripture, and learned men. Against that, she sets her own experience. Read her opening, then look at what she has done with it.

FROM THE PROLOGUE · MIDDLE ENGLISH, LINES 1–3
"Experience, though noon auctoritee
Were in this world, is right ynogh for me
To speke of wo that is in mariage…"
Gloss: Experience, even if no written authority existed in the world, is fully enough for me to speak about the woe that marriage brings. (noon auctoritee = no written authority; right ynogh = quite enough; wo = woe.)

Notice the confidence. She does not ask permission to speak; she declares that her life outranks the library. A few lines later she names her credential plainly: "Housbondes at chirche dore I have had fyve" (I have had five husbands at the church door). Five marriages become her evidence. That is a rhetorical move: turning a life into proof.

📚 PART 2 · THE POWER, AND THE RISK, OF THE FIRST PERSON

Letting a character narrate her own life is a gift and a trap, and Chaucer knows it. The gift: her voice is vivid, funny, and impossible to ignore. We hear her think. The trap: she is not a neutral witness. She is defending herself. Every fact she offers is a fact she has chosen to offer, shaped to win the argument she wants to win. When a speaker is also the person on trial, the reader has to hold two questions at once: What is she claiming? and What does she have to gain by claiming it?

This is the heart of reading rhetoric well. You are not deciding whether the Wife is likeable (she is). You are deciding whether her case is sound. A brilliant speaker can be right, wrong, or somewhere in between, and the brilliance itself tells you nothing about which. Keep that separation sharp all week.

📚 WORD STUDY
rhetoric — the art of persuasion; a speaker's deliberate choices to move an audience.  ·  auctoritee (authority) — the settled wisdom of books, scripture, and learned men.  ·  sovereignty — supreme power or self-rule; the right to decide for oneself.  ·  gentilesse (gentility) — true nobility of character.  ·  trouthe (faithfulness) — a pledged word; keeping faith.
Word origins: auctoritee comes from Latin auctor (originator), which also gives us author and authority. Gentilesse comes from Latin gentilis (of the family/clan) through French gentil, and gives us gentle, genteel, and gentleman. Trouthe is Old English treowth, the root of both truth and troth (as in "betroth").
Copia (in your Organizer): take the sentence "She won the argument." and rewrite it three times: once so the win comes from rhetoric, once so it comes from experience, and once so it comes from auctoritee. Notice how the source of the win changes what kind of victory it is.
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND PERSUASION
An AI tool can generate a fluent, confident paragraph arguing almost any position, including a weak one. That is worth remembering here: fluency is not proof. AI can help you paraphrase a hard Middle English line or list rhetorical devices, but deciding whether the Wife's case actually holds is judgment, and that stays with you.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

A speaker can be vivid, funny, and completely convincing, and still be arguing for herself. Read the Wife's rhetoric closely, then step back and ask the second question: does the case hold? Keep both questions open until her Tale is done.
