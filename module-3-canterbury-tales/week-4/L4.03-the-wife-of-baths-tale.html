📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A knight's life hangs on one question: what do women most desire? And the woman telling the story already knows her answer.
After a Prologue in her own voice, the Wife tells a story: an Arthurian tale of a crime, a quest, and a bargain with an old woman. Today you will follow the tale to its answer, sovereignty, and then ask the harder question this week has been building toward: is the tale a sincere argument, or a story rigged to prove its teller right?

📚 PART 1 · THE QUEST AND THE ANSWER
💭 BEFORE YOU READ · NO WRONG ANSWER
Before Chaucer answers it, answer it yourself: what do you think people most deeply want in a relationship: to be adored, to be understood, or to have a real say?

The setup: a crime and a strange mercy

The tale opens in King Arthur's days. A young knight of the court commits a terrible crime against a maiden, and the law condemns him to die. But the queen and her ladies intervene, and the king hands the judgment to them. The queen sets the knight a riddle instead of a sentence: he has a year and a day to discover what women most desire. If his answer fails, he dies. Notice who holds the power in this frame from the very first scene: the men pronounce the sentence, but the women decide what justice will be.

The answer: sovereignty

The knight searches for a year and finds no two people agree: some say wealth, some honor, some pleasure, some flattery. On the last day he meets an old woman, poor and, the tale insists, remarkably ugly. She offers him the true answer in exchange for a promise: he must grant whatever she asks next. Cornered, he pledges his trouthe, his sworn word. The answer she gives him saves his life before the queen's court:

FROM THE TALE · MIDDLE ENGLISH
"Wommen desyren to have sovereyntee
As wel over hir housbond as hir love…"
Gloss: Women desire to have sovereignty over their husband as well as their love. (sovereyntee = supreme rule, the final say; hir = their.)

Then the old woman calls in the debt: the knight must marry her. He begs her to take his goods and let his body go; she refuses. The promise was his trouthe, and in this tale a pledged word binds absolutely. The knight who broke faith with a woman by force must now keep faith with a woman by law.

📚 PART 2 · THE GENTILESSE SPEECH, THE CHOICE, AND THE QUESTION

The wedding-night sermon

On their wedding night the knight turns away in disgust: she is old, poor, and low-born. Her reply is the moral center of the tale, a long speech on gentilesse, true nobility. Rank and inherited wealth, she argues, cannot make a person noble. If gentilesse came with blood, the children of noble houses could never act shamefully, and they plainly do. Nobility is conduct, and its source is not ancestry:

FROM THE TALE · MIDDLE ENGLISH
"Thy gentillesse cometh fro God allone…
Than comth our verray gentillesse of grace"
Gloss: Your gentility comes from God alone; our true (verray) gentility comes of grace, not of old riches or noble birth.

Then she offers him a choice: she can remain old and ugly but faithful and true, or become young and beautiful and take her chances with what follows. The knight, apparently having learned the lesson of the year, gives the choice back to her: "I put me in your wyse governance" (I put myself under your wise governance). He yields the sovereignty. And the moment he does, the tale rewards him: she becomes both beautiful and faithful, and they live in perfect joy.

Sincere, or self-serving? Weigh it both ways

Here is where a careful reader slows down. The case that the tale is sincere: its argument is serious and consistent. The knight's crime was taking by force; his redemption is learning to yield. The gentilesse speech is real moral philosophy, and the tale's happy ending flows logically from the lesson. The case that it is self-serving: the teller is a woman who spent her whole Prologue boasting about winning the upper hand over five husbands. She invents a story in which a woman's rule is the answer to a royal riddle, an old wife gets a young husband, and yielding to one's wife is rewarded with a perfect marriage. The story proves exactly what its teller wants proved, down to the wish-fulfillment ending.

Both readings use real evidence, and Chaucer refuses to settle it for you. That refusal is the craft. In your notebook, before the widget below, write one piece of evidence for each side. You will need both for Discussion 4 and Quick Write 4.

✎ CHECK YOURSELF · PLACE EACH ITEM ON THE SCALE
Which reading does each piece of evidence support most strongly?
1. The tale's answer to the riddle is sovereignty, the very thing the Wife bragged about winning from her own husbands.
2. The gentilesse speech argues that nobility comes from conduct and grace, not birth or wealth, and gains the Wife nothing personally.
3. The tale is set in King Arthur's days, in a world of courts and quests.
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND OPEN QUESTIONS
Ask an AI tool "Is the Wife of Bath's Tale sincere?" and it will give you an answer, usually a balanced-sounding one. What it cannot do is weigh the evidence the way you just did, line by line, and commit to a reading you can defend. Six centuries of readers disagree about this tale. Your position, argued from the text, is the work.
