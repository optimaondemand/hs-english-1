GRAMMAR 4.02
Irony and Paradox: the Grammar of Contradiction
Lesson
🎯 TODAY'S FOCUS
How can a sentence say one thing and mean another, or hold two opposite ideas at once and still be true?
The Wife of Bath's argument runs on contradiction. To read her closely, and to write about her precisely, you need two tools with a clear difference between them: irony and paradox. You will learn to tell them apart, then build the sentence structures that carry contradiction on purpose.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 READ IT ALOUD · NO WRONG ANSWER
A friend trips, drops their lunch, and mutters, "Perfect. Just perfect." What are they actually telling you?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

Irony is a gap between what is said and what is meant, or between what seems true and what is true. When your friend says "Perfect" about a ruined lunch, that is verbal irony. When the General Prologue calls the Wife "a worthy womman al hir lyve" and then piles up details that complicate the praise, that is irony too: the surface and the reality do not match.

Paradox is different. A paradox is a statement that seems to contradict itself but, looked at closely, reveals a truth. "The more you give away, the more you have." It sounds impossible, yet it can be true of love or knowledge. The Wife is a walking paradox: she declares that marriage is nothing but wo (woe), and in the same breath goes looking for husband number six. The contradiction is the point; it tells you something real about her.

THE ONE DIFFERENCE TO HOLD ONTO
Irony = the words say one thing, the meaning is another (a gap).  Paradox = the statement contradicts itself on the surface, yet reveals a truth (a knot).

To build contradiction on purpose, writers reach for a small set of joining words. A contrast conjunction such as but, yet, although, though, while, or whereas lets one clause push against another inside a single sentence. That is the grammar of contradiction: two opposed ideas, framed so the tension shows.

ON THE BOARD · IRONY, PARADOX, AND THE JOINING WORDS
Irony: She called her fifth marriage “a blessed life,” then listed every quarrel in it.
Paradox: The more freedom he gave her, the less she needed to take.
Contrast sentence: Although she preaches against books, she quotes them constantly.
Contrast sentence: She claims marriage is only woe, yet she seeks a sixth husband.
The gold marks the contradiction. In the last two lines, the joining words although and yet hold the opposed ideas together in one sentence.
✎ YOUR TURN · DECIDE IN YOUR NOTEBOOK FIRST, THEN CLICK EACH CARD TO CHECK
CARD 1 · IRONY OR PARADOX?
"Standing in the pouring rain, she said, 'What a beautiful day for a walk.'"
Tap to reveal
ANSWER
Irony (verbal). The words praise the day; the meaning is the reverse. It is a gap between what is said and what is meant, not a self-contradiction that hides a truth.
CARD 2 · IRONY OR PARADOX?
"To keep her husband's love, she stopped trying to hold onto it."
Tap to reveal
ANSWER
Paradox. On the surface it contradicts itself (stop trying, to succeed), yet it reveals a truth about love and control. It is a knot, not a gap.
➡️ NOW WRITE YOUR OWN (IN YOUR ORGANIZER)

Write two sentences about the Wife of Bath. In the first, use a contrast conjunction (although, yet, whereas) to frame a real contradiction in her argument. In the second, write a paradox about her that could be true. Label which is which.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

Irony is a gap; paradox is a knot. Naming them precisely lets you describe exactly how the Wife's argument works, and a well-placed although or yet lets you build that tension into your own sentences.
