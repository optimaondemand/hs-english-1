GRAMMAR 1.02
The Appositive Phrase
Lesson
🎯 TODAY'S FOCUS
How can you tell your reader exactly who a person is, without stopping to add a whole new sentence?
You will learn what an appositive phrase is, how it renames the noun beside it, how to punctuate it, and how to use it the way Chaucer does: to pack a portrait full of identifying detail in a single smooth sentence.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 READ BOTH ALOUD · NO WRONG ANSWER
A. The Pardoner rode with the group. He was a seller of false relics.   |   B. The Pardoner, a seller of false relics, rode with the group.
Which version tells you who he is without pausing for a second sentence?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

An appositive is a noun or noun phrase placed right beside another noun to rename or explain it. In "The Knight, a true and noble soldier, had fought in many lands," the phrase a true and noble soldier renames the Knight. It is an appositive phrase. Chaucer builds his portraits this way, dropping an identifying phrase beside each pilgrim's name so we learn who they are as the sentence moves.

Most appositives are nonrestrictive: they add extra information you could remove and still have a complete sentence, so you set them off with commas. A few are restrictive, essential to the meaning, and take no commas, as in "the poet Chaucer" (which poet? the one named Chaucer).

ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
If the renaming phrase adds extra detail you could lift out, wrap it in commas. If removing it would change which person you mean, use no commas.
ON THE BOARD · SPOT THE APPOSITIVE
The Knight, a true and noble soldier, had fought in many lands.
Chaucer, a poet of the royal court, wrote in English.
The Pardoner, a seller of false relics, sang sweetly in church.
The gold underline marks the appositive phrase; the commas fence it off. Lift the gold out and each sentence still stands: that is why it takes commas.

➡️ NOW WRITE YOUR OWN (IN YOUR ORGANIZER)

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

An appositive lets you name a person and describe them in the same breath. Fence off the extra detail with commas, and your portraits gain Chaucer's density without losing their flow.
