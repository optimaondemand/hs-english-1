GRAMMAR 1.04
The Periodic Sentence
Lesson
🎯 TODAY'S FOCUS
How can a writer make you wait, holding the main idea back until the very last word?
You will learn what a periodic sentence is, how it differs from an ordinary loose sentence, and how Chaucer uses one enormous periodic sentence to open the whole Canterbury Tales.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 READ BOTH ALOUD · NO WRONG ANSWER
A. People long to travel when spring returns, the rains fall, and the birds sing.   |   B. When spring returns, when the rains fall, and when the birds sing, people long to travel.
Which version makes you wait for the main point until the end?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

A periodic sentence withholds its main clause (the completing idea) until the end. It opens with a stack of subordinate clauses or phrases that build up, and the meaning only clicks into place at the final word. Its opposite is the loose (or cumulative) sentence, which states the main idea first and then trails off into detail. The periodic sentence creates suspense and lands its emphasis on the last beat.

Chaucer opens the entire Canterbury Tales with a single periodic sentence eleven lines long. He describes April, the west wind, the young sun, the singing birds, and only at line 12 does the main clause finally arrive: Thanne longen folk to goon on pilgrimages (then people long to go on pilgrimages). All the spring imagery has been holding its breath, waiting for that main idea.

ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
Periodic sentence: build-up first, main clause last. Save the completing idea for the end, and the end hits harder.
ON THE BOARD · CHAUCER'S OPENING IS ONE PERIODIC SENTENCE
the build-up (subordinate clauses):
When April with its showers pierces the drought of March…
when the west wind breathes life into the tender shoots…
and the young sun has run half its course, and the small birds sing…
↓
the main clause, held to the end:
then people long to go on pilgrimages.
The gold is the main clause. Everything above it merely prepares the ground. Chaucer makes eleven lines of springtime wait for one plain human wish.
✎ YOUR TURN · CHOOSE THE BEST ANSWER

➡️ NOW WRITE YOUR OWN (IN YOUR ORGANIZER)

Write one periodic sentence about a journey of your own. Stack at least two "when" or "because" clauses first, and hold your main clause, the thing the traveler finally does or wants, for the very last words.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

A periodic sentence makes the reader wait, and the wait is the effect. When you want a line to land with weight, hold the main idea for the end, the way Chaucer holds his whole spring for one word: pilgrimages.
