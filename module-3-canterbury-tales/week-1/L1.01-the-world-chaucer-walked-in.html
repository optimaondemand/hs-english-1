📚 PART 1 · THE POET AND HIS ENGLAND

Who was Geoffrey Chaucer?

Geoffrey Chaucer (about A.D. 1343 to 1400) was not a knight or a lord. He was a working man of the middle rank: the son of a London wine merchant who rose to serve the king as a diplomat, a customs official on the London docks, and a clerk of the royal works. That job sent him up and down the social ladder, from the palace to the wharves, and it shows. Chaucer had met every kind of person he writes about. His great achievement, The Canterbury Tales, was begun in the late 1380s and left unfinished at his death. Most daring of all, he wrote it in English. In his day, serious literature was written in Latin or French; English was the rough tongue of ordinary people. By choosing it, Chaucer helped turn English into a language fit for great writing.

A world shaken and sorted: the three estates

Medieval people pictured society as three great estates, or classes, each with a God-given job. Those who pray (the clergy) cared for souls. Those who fight (the nobility) defended the land. Those who work (the peasants and laborers) fed everyone. In theory the three fit together like parts of one body. In Chaucer's lifetime, though, the picture was cracking. The Black Death of 1348 to 1349 had killed roughly a third of England, leaving fewer workers who could suddenly demand better pay, and a rising class of merchants and craftspeople who fit none of the three neat boxes. Chaucer's pilgrims come from all of it, and several of them, like the wealthy Wife of Bath or the grasping Pardoner, refuse to stay in their assigned place.

HOW MEDIEVAL ENGLAND PICTURED SOCIETY · THE THREE ESTATES
🙏
First Estate
Those who pray
The clergy: monks, priests, nuns, pardoners. In the Tales: the Prioress, the Monk, the Pardoner.
⚔️
Second Estate
Those who fight
The nobility and warriors. In the Tales: the Knight and his son the Squire.
🌾
Third Estate
Those who work
Peasants, laborers, and the rising trades. In the Tales: the Plowman, the Miller, the Cook.
The neat model left people out. Merchants, guildsmen, and a self-made woman like the Wife of Bath belonged to a new middle rank the three estates could not contain. Chaucer's road makes room for them all.
📚 PART 2 · ONE ROAD, MANY LIVES: THE FRAME

The pilgrimage as a framing device

A pilgrimage is a journey to a holy place. Chaucer's pilgrims are traveling to the shrine of Saint Thomas Becket at Canterbury Cathedral. Becket, an archbishop, had been murdered there in A.D. 1170, and for two centuries afterward crowds walked to his tomb to pray for healing. That journey was one of the few occasions when a knight, a nun, a miller, and a merchant might travel side by side for days.

Chaucer turns that journey into a framing device: an outer story that holds many inner stories. The pilgrims meet at the Tabard Inn in Southwark, and the innkeeper, the Host, proposes a contest. Each pilgrim will tell tales on the road, and the best storyteller wins a free supper. The frame is a machine for revelation. Because everyone must speak, everyone exposes themselves, and the tale a pilgrim chooses tells us as much about the teller as about the story.

Three tools for reading character: portrait, irony, satire

PortraitA vivid word-picture of a person, built from concrete details: dress, habits, speech. Chaucer paints one for each pilgrim in the General Prologue.
IronyA gap between what is said and what is meant, or between how a person appears and what they truly are. Chaucer's narrator often "praises" a pilgrim in words that quietly expose a flaw.
SatireWriting that exposes human folly or vice, often through humor, to hold it up for judgment. Chaucer aims his sharpest satire at corruption in the Church.

📚 WORD STUDY
estates · the three great classes of medieval society.  ·  pilgrimage · a journey to a holy place.  ·  framing device · an outer story that contains other stories.  ·  portrait · a word-picture of a person made of concrete details.  ·  irony · a gap between what is said and what is meant.  ·  satire · writing that exposes folly or vice to judgment.
Word origins: pilgrimage traces back to Latin peregrinus, "one from abroad, a foreigner" (from per ager, "through the fields"): a pilgrim is literally a traveler far from home. Satire comes from Latin satura, a "full dish" or medley of mixed ingredients, because the form mixes many targets together. Estate comes from Latin status, "standing" or "condition."
Copia (in your Organizer): take the plain sentence "The pardoner gave the coins to the church." Rewrite it twice: once so the detail reads as sincere devotion (a flattering portrait), and once so the same act reads as hollow show (dripping with irony). Change only the surrounding words, not the action.
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND CONTEXT
An AI tool can give you a quick timeline of medieval England or a one-line definition of "the three estates," which can be a fair way to check a fact. It cannot tell you whether Chaucer admires a pilgrim or is quietly mocking him. That judgment comes from reading the details yourself, and it stays your work.
