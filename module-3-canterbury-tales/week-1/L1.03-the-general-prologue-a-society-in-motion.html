📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
When a narrator praises everyone he meets, how do you find out who is actually worth admiring?
Today you meet Chaucer's pilgrims. You will hear the famous opening of the General Prologue in Chaucer's own Middle English, then study five portraits (the Knight, the Prioress, the Wife of Bath, the Miller, and the Pardoner) and learn to catch the irony hiding inside a compliment.

📚 PART 1 · THE OPENING: SPRING SETS THE ROAD IN MOTION
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Think of the last time warm weather made you restless to go somewhere. What is it about spring that pushes people out the door?

Chaucer opens not with a person but with the season. In one long, unspooling sentence, he describes April rains waking the earth, the west wind breathing life into the fields, birds singing through the night, and only then arrives at his real subject: when spring stirs the world, people long to go on pilgrimages. The rhythm of the verse is iambic pentameter, ten beats to a line, and the lines fall into rhymed pairs called couplets. Read the original below, then use the modern gloss beside it.

ON THE BOARD · THE GENERAL PROLOGUE, LINES 1–18 (MIDDLE ENGLISH)
Whan that Aprille with his shoures soote
The droghte of March hath perced to the roote,
And bathed every veyne in swich licour
Of which vertu engendred is the flour;
Whan Zephirus eek with his sweete breeth
Inspired hath in every holt and heeth
The tendre croppes, and the yonge sonne
Hath in the Ram his halfe cours yronne,
And smale foweles maken melodye,
That slepen al the nyght with open ye
(So priketh hem Nature in hir corages),
Thanne longen folk to goon on pilgrimages,
And palmeres for to seken straunge strondes,
To ferne halwes, kowthe in sondry londes;
And specially, from every shires ende
Of Engelond, to Caunterbury they wende,
The hooly blisful martir for to seke,
That hem hath holpen whan that they were seeke.
Modern gloss: When April with its sweet showers has pierced the drought of March to the root, and bathed every vein of the plants in the moisture whose power brings forth the flower; when the west wind too, with his sweet breath, has breathed life into the tender shoots in every wood and field, and the young sun has run half its course through the sign of the Ram, and small birds make melody who sleep all night with open eyes (so Nature stirs them in their hearts), then people long to go on pilgrimages, and pilgrims to seek far-off shores and distant shrines known in various lands; and especially, from every shire's end of England, they travel to Canterbury to seek the holy blessed martyr who helped them when they were sick.
The gold words at the line ends are the rhymes: the lines pair off two by two into couplets (soote / roote, licour / flour, breeth / heeth…), each line carrying five beats of iambic pentameter.
Read the full General Prologue in the public-domain Middle English edition (Project Gutenberg, edited by W. W. Skeat): gutenberg.org/ebooks/2383. A modern-English reading edition may be provided in your Canvas module; use it alongside the original.
📚 PART 2 · FIVE PORTRAITS, AND THE IRONY INSIDE THEM

After the opening, Chaucer the narrator introduces the company one by one. He sounds impressed by nearly everyone. That is the trap. A careful reader watches the details, because the details often reveal a truth the cheerful narrator seems to miss. Here are five pilgrims and the single detail worth pausing on in each.

The Knight · the genuine article
Chaucer calls him "a verray, parfit gentil knyght" (a true, perfect, noble knight). He has fought honorably in many campaigns, speaks little, and wears a tunic still stained from his armor. Here the praise is straight: this portrait carries little or no irony. Chaucer shows you the real thing first, so you can measure the others against it.
The Prioress · devotion, or manners?
A prioress is the head of a convent, sworn to humility. Yet Chaucer lingers on her table manners, her French, her pet dogs, and a gold brooch reading "Amor vincit omnia" (Love conquers all). Every detail is worldly refinement where we expect spiritual devotion. The gap is gentle irony: she is charming, but is she holy?
The Wife of Bath · experience over authority
Chaucer notes she has had "housbondes at chirche dore… fyve" (five husbands, married at the church door), dresses richly, is gap-toothed, and has traveled on many pilgrimages herself. She is bold, worldly, and utterly sure of her own experience: a woman who refuses the quiet place her society assigns her.
The Miller · coarse and dishonest
Brawny and loud, with a beard "reed" as a sow or fox and a wart on his nose, the Miller cheats his customers, taking more grain than he is owed. Chaucer barely hides the judgment here: the ugly outside matches the dishonest inside, and the satire is close to the surface.
The Pardoner · Chaucer's sharpest satire
A pardoner sold official Church pardons. This one also peddles fake relics (pig bones passed off as saints' bones) and sings sweetly in church to loosen people's purses. Chaucer aims his most cutting satire at him: a man who sells forgiveness he does not possess. Corruption dressed as holiness.

📚 WORD STUDY
prioress · the woman who leads a convent of nuns.  ·  relic · a holy object, such as a saint's bone, honored by the faithful.  ·  couplet · a pair of rhymed lines.  ·  gloss · a translation or explanation placed beside a hard text.
Word origins: relic comes from Latin reliquiae, "remains, things left behind." Gloss comes from Greek glossa, "tongue, language": a gloss lends its tongue to a text you cannot yet read.
Copia (in your Organizer): take one flattering line about a pilgrim, such as "She was a most refined and gracious lady." Rewrite it once so the refinement reads as true virtue, and once so it reads as empty polish, the way Chaucer tilts the Prioress. Then add each of today's pilgrims to your Character Tracking Chart.
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND MIDDLE ENGLISH
An AI tool can translate a Middle English line into modern English quickly, which can help when a word stumps you. It cannot decide for you whether a detail is a sincere compliment or a quiet jab. That reading is a judgment, and judgment is the skill this module is building in you.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

Chaucer rarely tells you what to think. He shows you a detail and lets it do the work. Learn to read the detail, and you can read the soul it exposes. That is the whole method of the General Prologue.
