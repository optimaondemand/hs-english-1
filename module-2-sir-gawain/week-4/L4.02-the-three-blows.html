📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
The Green Knight owes Gawain one blow. Why does he swing three times?
The scene at the Green Chapel looks like an execution. It is actually a verdict. Today you will see that the three axe strokes are not suspense for its own sake: each one is the Green Knight's report card on one of the three days back at the castle. Read the blows correctly and the whole poem reorganizes itself in front of you.

📚 PART 1 · WHAT HAPPENS AT THE BLOCK
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Think of a teacher, coach, or referee who tests you by pretending, faking a throw, holding back to see if you flinch. Why do people test that way instead of just asking?

Gawain kneels and bows his neck to the block. The Green Knight raises the axe. On the first swing, Gawain flinches, his shoulders shrink from the blade, and the Green Knight halts and mocks him: this cannot be the Gawain whose courage is famous. Stung, Gawain promises he will not flinch again, and adds that unlike the Green Knight, he cannot put his head back on once it falls. On the second swing, the Green Knight brings the axe down and stops it on purpose, testing whether Gawain will hold still. He does. Now the Green Knight praises his steady heart and prepares the true stroke. On the third swing, the axe finally falls, and it only nicks the side of Gawain's neck, cutting the skin so that a little blood drops to the snow. Gawain leaps up, seizes his sword, and declares the covenant fulfilled: one blow was the whole bargain, and he will take no more.

📚 PART 2 · WHY THREE? EACH BLOW IS A DAY

Here is the reveal that reorganizes the poem. Back at the castle, Gawain and his host struck a bargain: for three days the lord would hunt and give Gawain his winnings, and Gawain would give the lord whatever he gained inside the castle. On Days 1 and 2, Gawain honestly handed over the kisses the lady had given him. On Day 3, she also gave him a green girdle she claimed would protect his life, and Gawain kept it and hid it. The Green Knight is that same lord. His three swings are an exact accounting of those three days: two harmless feints for the two days Gawain was faithful, and one cut for the one day he was not.

Notice what the poet chooses not to do. He does not stop the action to have a narrator explain the moral. He lets the structure carry the meaning: three blows for three days, two spared and one paid. This is the same design you traced in Week 3, when the hunts and temptations ran in parallel. The poem rewards the reader who counts. If you saw the pattern of three, the verdict is already legible in the swings themselves, before anyone says a word.

⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND STRUCTURE
An AI tool can tell you that the poem repeats the number three, which is a real and checkable fact. It cannot feel the click of recognition when you realize the third swing is Day 3 coming due. Noticing how a structure delivers meaning is a reader's skill, and it is the one you are building right now.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

The Green Knight owed one blow and delivered a verdict in three. Two feints and a nick tell the whole story of the three days: Gawain was almost perfectly faithful, and the small cut is the exact measure of his one small failure. The poem argues with its shape, not its speeches.
