📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
If someone offered you a safe, secret way out of keeping your word, and swore no one would ever know, would you take it?
Fitt IV opens with Gawain riding toward almost certain death. On the way, his guide makes him an offer that is harder to refuse than any monster. Today you will read fear as a moral event, not just a feeling, and watch how the poet turns a lonely ride into the real test of the poem.

📚 PART 1 · FEAR AS A MORAL EVENT
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Think of a time you were genuinely afraid of doing something you had promised to do. What did the fear tempt you to do?

A year of dread, then the ride

A year ago Gawain struck the Green Knight's head from his shoulders, and the Green Knight picked it up and rode away, promising to return the blow. Now the year is spent. On New Year's morning Gawain arms himself, mounts his horse Gringolet, and rides out into a frozen landscape to find the Green Chapel and kneel for a stroke he believes will kill him. Notice what the poet does here: he does not hide Gawain's fear or pretend a true knight feels none. Fear is present the whole way. The question the poem asks is not whether Gawain is afraid. It is what he does while afraid. That is what turns fear from a feeling into a moral event: a place where character is decided.

📚 PART 2 · WHY THE OFFER IS THE REAL TEST

A monster tests your strength. The guide tests your honesty when no one is looking. His offer removes every external consequence: no witnesses, no shame, no report carried home. Everything that usually keeps a person honorable is stripped away, and what remains is only Gawain's own sense of who he is. This is why the ride, not the axe, is the poem's turning point. Honor that depends on being watched is not honor. It is reputation. The poem quietly asks whether Gawain's honor is the real thing.

Gawain refuses. He tells the guide he would be a coward knight if he fled, and that he trusts God to do with him as He will. He rides on to the chapel, which turns out to be no church at all: he finds "a mound on a level space of land by a bank beside the stream where it ran swiftly, the water bubbled within as if boiling." He hears a blade being whetted on a grindstone across the water. Keep this moment in view: Gawain has already passed the harder test before the axe is ever raised. Hold that thought for Lesson 4.03, when he judges himself far more harshly than this ride would suggest.

📚 WORD STUDY
execration — a curse; the act of cursing something bitterly.  ·  dissemble — to hide one's true feelings or intentions; to disguise.  ·  remiss — careless or negligent in a duty.  ·  notorious — widely known, especially for something bad.  ·  indignant — angry at something felt to be unfair or unworthy.
Word origins: dissemble comes from Latin dissimulare, "to hide, to make unlike," built from simulare, "to make like" (the root of simulate). To dissemble is to make yourself unlike what you truly are. Notorious comes from Latin notus, "known"; the same root gives us note and notice. The Green Knight is notorious precisely because everyone has taken notice of him.
Copia (in your Organizer): take the sentence "Gawain was afraid, but he rode on." Work one Word Study term into at least one version.
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND MORAL READING
An AI tool can define "moral courage" or list themes in the poem in seconds, and that can help you check a fact. It cannot decide for you whether Gawain's choice on that lonely road counts as brave, or what you yourself would do in his place. That judgment is the work of this lesson, and it stays yours.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

The poem does not ask whether Gawain feels fear. It asks what he does with it. On the ride to the Green Chapel, offered a safe and secret escape, he keeps his word. Remember that when Gawain later condemns himself: the poem has already shown you a braver man than he will admit to being.
