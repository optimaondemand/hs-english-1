📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
One small cut. Three very different verdicts. Whose is right?
Gawain survives, but the wound to his pride runs deeper than the nick on his neck. Today you will read his fierce self-judgment, learn who the lord of the castle really was, and see how three parties reach three different conclusions about the same green girdle. This gap between the verdicts is exactly where your essay will live.

📚 PART 1 · THE REVELATION AND THE CONFESSION
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Have you ever been forgiven for something quickly, and still could not forgive yourself? What does that gap tell you?

After the third blow, the Green Knight lowers his axe and reveals the truth: he is the lord of the castle where Gawain was a guest, and the whole beheading game was a test arranged to try the honor of the Round Table. He had set his own wife to tempt Gawain for three days, and Gawain, he says, came through almost perfectly. He failed only in keeping the green girdle, and even that, the Green Knight insists, was no act of lust or greed but a very human wish to stay alive. For that, he says, Gawain deserves little blame. Notice the word from Lesson 4.01 at work here: the lord had to dissemble, to hide who he was, for the test to be real.

Gawain does not accept the mercy. He is overcome with shame. He tears at the girdle, throws it down, and pours out a bitter self-judgment, an execration, a curse, on the fault that undid him. He names two vices by name: cowardice, because fear for his life made him take the girdle, and covetousness, the grasping desire that made him keep it. He confesses he was remiss, faithless to the bargain, and calls himself false. Then he makes a striking decision: he will keep the girdle after all, not as a prize but as a permanent badge of his failure, to wear so he can never forget the moment his perfect honor cracked.

📚 PART 2 · THREE VERDICTS ON ONE GIRDLE

Here is what makes the ending so rich, and so useful for your essay. The same small act, keeping a green girdle, is judged three different ways by three different parties. The poem does not tell you which verdict is correct. It sets all three in front of you and steps back.

Back at Camelot, Gawain tells the whole story, shows the scar, and holds up the girdle as "the token of the untruth" he was caught in. The court's response is not what he expects: they laugh, not in cruelty but in warmth, and turn his private shame into a shared emblem. Is that wisdom, generously refusing to crush a good man for one lapse? Or is it evasion, a court that cannot bear to look honestly at failure? The girdle now means at least three things at once: guilt, mercy, and honor. Which meaning wins is the argument you will make.

📚 WORD STUDY · COPIA WITH DISSEMBLE
The lord had to dissemble, to hide his true identity, for the test to work; the lady dissembled her real purpose in each visit. In your Organizer, take the sentence "The lord dissembled his identity to test Gawain." Rewrite it three ways: once substituting a synonym for dissembled (for example, disguised, concealed, masked); once altering the syntax so the sentence opens with a participial phrase (for example, "Dissembling his identity, the lord…"); once changing the register so it sounds like a modern news report. Keep the meaning of dissemble exact in all three.
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND JUDGMENT
An AI tool can list the three verdicts on Gawain and even summarize the arguments for each. It cannot decide which verdict is truest, because that is a judgment about honor, mercy, and human weakness. Weighing those three verdicts and choosing is the exact skill your disputatio essay asks of you.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

The poem refuses to hand you a single verdict. Gawain condemns himself, Bertilak forgives him, and the court celebrates him, all over one green girdle. Your essay steps into that gap. The disagreement is not a flaw in the poem. It is the poem's invitation to you.
