📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Before Gawain rides into danger, the poet paints a five-pointed star on his shield. Why start a quest with a diagram of virtue?
In Fitt II, as Gawain is armed, the Pearl Poet pauses on the emblem he carries: a five-pointed star the poet calls the pentangle, or "endless knot." Each point stands for a set of five ideals Gawain is bound to keep. Today you will read that passage closely and see the whole standard Gawain will be measured against for the rest of the poem.

📚 PART 1 · A SHIELD MADE OF IDEALS
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Suppose you had to sum up the person you most want to be in exactly five words. What is one word that would make your list?

The endless knot

When Gawain is fully armed, the poet turns to his shield and the golden star painted on it. He calls it a sign "that Solomon set ere-while, as betokening truth," a figure "with five points" where "each line overlaps the other," so that it has neither beginning nor end. For that reason, the poet says, "in English it is called 'the endless knot.'" The shape matters as much as the meaning: a line that never lifts and never breaks. To keep the pentangle is to keep all of it at once, with nothing left out and nothing dropped. That is a demanding promise, and the poem will spend three more Fitts testing whether any human being can keep it.

The poet then unfolds the star into five groups of five. Gawain is "faultless in his five senses"; his "five fingers never failed him"; his faith rests on "the five wounds that Christ bare"; his courage draws on "the five joys which the Queen of Heaven had of her Child"; and the fifth five is a set of five virtues that "never failed him." It is this last group that the poem really cares about, because these are the qualities a knight must show toward other people.

📚 PART 2 · WHAT EACH POINT DEMANDS

Read across the star and one thing stands out: only one of the five virtues is about the knight himself. The other four are about how he treats other people. A knight of the pentangle is not just brave. He is generous, loyal, clean in conduct, well-mannered, and merciful, all at once and without exception. Here is what each point asks:

GenerosityGiving freely, without holding back for yourself. The poet's word is frankness.
FellowshipLoyalty to companions; keeping faith with the people you are bound to.
PurityCleanness of conduct; being honest and undivided in what you do.
CourtesyGrace and respect in how you treat others, high or low.
CompassionMercy and pity; care for those who suffer. The poet's word is pity.

Notice the trap the poet is setting. A code with one virtue is easy: be brave, and you pass. A code with five, tied in an endless knot, means a single failure in a single point unties the whole thing. Keep this in mind as you read on. The poem is about to send this five-pointed man into a world where the virtues start to pull against each other.

📚 WORD STUDY
piety: dutiful devotion to God, family, and duty (this is close to the poet's pity at the fifth point).  ·  manifold: many and varied; of many kinds at once.  ·  mien: a person's outward bearing, the manner they carry themselves.  ·  surpassing: exceeding all others; going beyond the ordinary.  ·  inviolate: unbroken and untouched; kept pure, never violated.
Word origins: piety comes from Latin pietas, the sense of duty a Roman owed to gods and family; mien arrived through French mine, the look on a face; inviolate joins Latin in- ("not") to violare ("to violate"), so it literally means "not broken into." Notice that Gawain's endless knot is meant to stay exactly that: inviolate.
