📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Gawain finds warmth, feasting, and a smiling host. So why should a careful reader feel nervous?
At the end of his winter ride, Gawain reaches a dazzling castle. Its lord welcomes him warmly and, in a moment that seems like harmless holiday fun, proposes a game: they will trade whatever each of them wins in the coming days. Today you will read the castle's arrival and see how this "exchange-of-winnings" bargain sets the trap for Fitt III.

📚 PART 1 · A CASTLE TOO GOOD TO BE TRUE
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
You are cold, exhausted, and lost, and a stranger offers you a warm place to stay and a game to pass the time. What is your first instinct?

The castle appears

The moment Gawain finishes his prayer, the poet gives us "the fairest castle that ever a knight owned," set "in a meadow with a park all about it." Its towers are so delicate that its "pinnacles" seem "as if it were all wrought out of paper." That last image is worth pausing on. Paper is beautiful and fragile; it looks solid but tears easily. The castle is a place of comfort and courtesy, yet the poet has quietly warned us that appearances here may not be as firm as they seem. Inside, the lord of the castle greets Gawain with open warmth, and the halls fill with feasting and good cheer.

This is hospitality, one of the great values of the medieval world: the sacred duty to welcome and protect a guest. But hospitality runs two ways. A guest, too, owes his host courtesy and good faith. That mutual bond is exactly what the lord is about to turn into a game.

📚 PART 2 · THE EXCHANGE-OF-WINNINGS GAME

The lord tells Gawain to rest in the castle while he rides out to hunt each day. Then he proposes a bargain, framed as festive fun: "Whatsoever I win in the wood shall be yours, and whatever may fall to your share, that shall ye exchange for it." In plain terms, each evening the two men will swap whatever they have gained that day. The lord will hand over his hunt; Gawain will hand over whatever comes to him inside the castle. Gawain, ever courteous, agrees. It sounds like a harmless holiday deal. It is, in fact, the mechanism of his central test.

📚 WORD STUDY · COPIA WITH "SURPASSING"
surpassing: exceeding all others; going beyond the ordinary. The castle's beauty is surpassing: it is the fairest Gawain has ever seen.
Copia (in your Organizer): start from "The castle's beauty was surpassing." Rewrite it three ways: (1) swap in a synonym for "surpassing"; (2) change the syntax so the sentence opens differently (for example, "Beyond any hall Gawain knew…"); (3) shift the register so it sounds plain and modern, then again so it sounds like the poet. Keep all three versions in your notes.
