📚 PART 1 · THE POEM AND ITS WORLD
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Think of a code of conduct you already know: a team's rules, a family's expectations, a job's standards. When someone follows it perfectly, how do you feel about them? What happens when they break it?

Who wrote it, and when?

We do not know the poet's name. Sir Gawain and the Green Knight was composed in the late 1300s, around the same years Geoffrey Chaucer was writing The Canterbury Tales far to the south. Its anonymous author, usually called the Pearl Poet after another poem in the same handwriting, wrote in a northwest-Midlands dialect of Middle English that can look strange on the page even to readers who know Chaucer. The poem survives in a single small manuscript, now kept in the British Library, that came close to being lost in a fire. One copy: that is all that stood between us and never knowing this poem at all.

The 14th century was not a gentle age. England was locked in a long war with France, and in 1348 the plague known as the Black Death killed a huge share of the population. Yet this was also the world that produced a poem obsessed with courtesy, loyalty, and keeping one's word. The poet looks back to the legendary court of King Arthur, already ancient and half-imagined, as a place where the ideal of the knight still shone. That backward glance matters: the poem admires chivalry and quietly asks whether anyone could ever live up to it.

📚 PART 2 · THE CHIVALRIC CODE AND THE ARTHURIAN TRADITION

What chivalry asked of a knight

Chivalry was the code of conduct expected of a knight. The word comes from the French for "horseman," but it meant far more than skill in battle. A true knight was to be brave, but also loyal to his lord, honest in his word, courteous to all, generous to the weak, and devout toward God. When a knight made a promise, that promise became a covenant, a binding agreement he was bound in honor to keep, whatever it cost him. The rude, ill-mannered behavior a knight was supposed to rise above had its own name: to act churlish was to behave like a low, coarse person with no courtesy at all.

CourageFace danger without flinching, even a danger you might not survive.
LoyaltyKeep faith with your lord and your word, above your own comfort.
CourtesyShow grace and good manners to everyone, high or low, friend or stranger.
PietyHonor God and the Church, and act as though heaven is watching.
GenerosityProtect the weak and give freely, never hoarding safety for yourself.

Why Arthur, again and again?

King Arthur was not invented by our poet. For centuries, writers across Britain and France had told and retold the story of a British king and his Round Table of knights. By the 1300s, Arthur's court stood for a golden age of honor that every later court measured itself against. When the Pearl Poet sets his story there, he is borrowing that shine on purpose. Arthur can be imperious, quick to command and eager for a marvel to break the feast; his court is proud of its own perfection. Into that self-satisfied brilliance the poet sends a stranger who will not be easily appeased, and the testing begins.

📚 WORD STUDY · THIS WEEK'S FIVE
chivalry — the code of honor and conduct expected of a knight.  ·  covenant — a solemn, binding agreement.  ·  churlish — rude, mean, and lacking manners.  ·  imperious — commanding in a proud, domineering way.  ·  appease — to calm or satisfy, to bring to peace.
Word origins: covenant comes from Latin convenire, "to come together," so a covenant is where two wills meet and agree. Churlish grows from Old English ceorl, a common peasant; over time it came to name the rough manners once linked with low birth. Appease traces back through French to Latin pax, "peace," the same root as pacify.
Copia (in your Organizer): take the sentence "The stranger spoke rudely to the king," and rewrite it twice: once so the stranger sounds clearly churlish, and once so the king sounds imperious in reply. Same event, two very different tones.
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND HISTORICAL CONTEXT
An AI tool can quickly list facts about the 14th century, and that can be a useful way to check a date. It cannot decide for you what those facts mean for this poem, or notice that a plague-scarred age chose to write about perfect courtesy. Connecting the world to the words is the reading work, and it stays yours.
