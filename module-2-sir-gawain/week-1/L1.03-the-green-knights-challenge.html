📚 PART 1 · AN UNINVITED GUEST
💭 BEFORE WE LOOK CLOSE · NO WRONG ANSWER
A stranger interrupts a celebration and dares anyone present to accept a dangerous game, in front of everyone. If no one steps up, what happens to the group's reputation?

The scene

It is New Year's Day at Camelot, in the middle of a fifteen-day feast. Arthur, restless and young, refuses to eat until he has heard some marvel. He gets one. A giant knight rides straight into the hall, "green all over," on a green horse, carrying a holly branch in one hand and a huge axe in the other. He is enormous, richly dressed, and completely calm. He asks for the ruler of the court, and then makes his offer, which he insists is only "a Christmas jest."

❝ FROM THE POEM · THE CHALLENGE (WESTON, FITT I)
“If any one in this hall holds himself so hardy, so bold both of blood and brain, as to dare strike me one stroke for another, I will give him as a gift this axe… and I will abide the first blow, unarmed as I sit.”
The catch comes next: whoever strikes him must seek him out in a year and a day to receive a blow in return. “The respite of a year and a day shall he have.”

For a long moment, no one moves. The Green Knight sneers at the silence, taunting the court's famous reputation. Arthur himself, stung, seizes the axe, and then Gawain quietly asks to take his place, calling himself the weakest and least important of the knights, so that the loss would be smallest if things went wrong. It is a strikingly humble way to volunteer for something terrifying.

❝ GAWAIN ACCEPTS (WESTON, FITT I)
“Gawain am I, who give thee this buffet, let what may come of it; and at this time twelvemonth will I take another at thine hand with whatsoever weapon thou wilt.”
📚 PART 2 · THE COVENANT AND THE BLOW

What makes this a test of honor and not just a stunt is the covenant: a binding agreement, sealed with a promise. Gawain does not merely swing the axe. He gives his word to return, unarmed, for a matching blow. The whole game is really a trap for one virtue: will Gawain keep a promise even when keeping it looks like walking to his own death? Here is the shape of the bargain.

Read the aftermath in Weston's words. Notice how the poet refuses to let the horror be simply grim; the headless knight is almost matter-of-fact.

❝ THE BLOW (WESTON, FITT I)
“The blood spurted forth, and glistened on the green raiment, but the knight neither faltered nor fell; he started forward with out-stretched hand, and caught the head, and lifted it up.”

A word to weigh: churlish

Part of what unsettles the court is that the Green Knight is hard to place. He is not simply churlish, rude and low-born, though he mocks them; nor is he simply courteous. He seems to be testing whether Camelot's manners are real or just for show.

📚 COPIA (IN YOUR ORGANIZER) · churlish
Take the sentence "The knight spoke to the court." Rewrite it three ways: (1) so the knight sounds churlish; (2) so he sounds coldly courteous; (3) so he sounds openly mocking. Keep the same basic event; change only the tone. Then note which version best fits the Green Knight, and why.

⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND CLOSE READING
An AI tool can give you a quick plot summary of this scene. It cannot feel the strangeness of a headless knight calmly lifting his own head, or decide what that image means for honor. Sitting with a difficult passage until it opens up is the reading work, and it stays yours.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

The beheading game is not really about the axe. It is about a promise. From the first Fitt, the poem is asking whether Gawain's honor is strong enough to make him keep his word all the way to the Green Chapel.
