📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
This poem does not rhyme at the ends of its lines. So what holds it together and makes it pound like a drum?
The Pearl Poet writes in an old English rhythm built not on end-rhyme but on alliteration: repeated opening sounds hammered across the strong beats of a line. You will learn how the alliterative long line works, meet the poem's surprising bob-and-wheel ending, and mark the beats yourself.

The alliterative long line

Most poems we hear today rhyme at the ends of lines. This poem does something older. Each long line is built on alliteration: the repetition of the same opening consonant sound across the line's strongest beats. Every line has four heavy beats, called lifts, with a short pause called a caesura in the middle. Usually the first three lifts share one sound, and the fourth deliberately breaks it, so the line lands with a small thud of change. Read aloud, the effect is muscular and driving, like a hammer striking metal. That pounding sound is not decoration. It carries the force of the story.

Here is a real line from the poem, in its original Middle English, describing the Green Knight himself. Listen for the three beats on the sound of g, the very sound at the front of the word green.

📚 PART 2 · NOW YOU MARK THE BEATS

You just saw a line worked out for you. Now try one yourself. Here is the poem's very first line, which reaches back to the fall of Troy. Read it aloud slowly, and listen for the beats that share one opening sound.

“Siþen þe sege and þe assaut watz sesed at Troye”
Modern English: "Since the siege and the assault was ceased at Troy."
In your Organizer: write the line out and underline the words whose stressed beats share the same opening sound. Then say which sound it is, and whether the last strong beat joins the pattern or breaks it.

When you have marked it, test your ear against the questions below.

💡 WHY THE SOUND MATTERS
This meter was made to be heard. Medieval audiences did not read the poem silently; they listened as it was read aloud. The alliteration made lines easier to remember and gave the storytelling a heavy, forward-driving pulse, perfect for a scene where a giant green stranger comes crashing into a feast. When you meet the Green Knight next lesson, read him aloud and feel the beats land.
