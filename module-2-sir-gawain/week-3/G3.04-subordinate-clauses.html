Subordinate Clauses: The Syntax of Delay
Lesson
🎯 TODAY'S FOCUS
How can the order of your clauses make a reader lean forward and wait?
You already know a subordinate clause cannot stand alone. Today you will use that fact on purpose: stack subordinate clauses at the front of a sentence to delay the main point and build suspense, the way the poet holds you across three tense mornings before the girdle appears.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 READ BOTH ALOUD · NO WRONG ANSWER
A. Gawain took the girdle.   |   B. When the lady swore that no man who wore it could be slain, when the blow was one day away, and when his own life hung on it, Gawain took the girdle.
Which version makes you feel the weight of the decision before it lands?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

A subordinate clause has a subject and a verb but cannot stand alone, because it begins with a subordinating conjunction such as when, although, because, since, if, or while. On its own it leaves the reader hanging: When the lady returned… what? That hanging feeling is useful. If you place one or more subordinate clauses before the main clause, you force the reader to hold the tension until the main clause finally resolves it. Writers call the result a suspenseful, or periodic, sentence. The longer the delay, the harder the main clause lands.

ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
Front-loaded subordinate clauses create suspense. Each one takes a comma; the main clause lands last, and it lands hardest.
ON THE BOARD · DELAY THE MAIN CLAUSE TO BUILD SUSPENSE
While her husband chased the deer, the lady came softly to his chamber.
Because the blow drew near, and because he feared for his life, Gawain hid the girdle.
When at last the third morning came, everything he had held would be tested.
The gold underline marks each subordinate clause. Notice how the main clause waits until the end, so the reader waits with it.

➡️ NOW WRITE YOUR OWN (IN YOUR ORGANIZER)

Write three complex sentences about a tense moment in Fitt III. In each, put at least one subordinate clause first to delay the main clause and build suspense; use a different subordinating conjunction each time (for example when, although, because), and remember the comma.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

Where you put the main clause controls when the reader can relax. Delay it behind subordinate clauses, and you make your reader wait, exactly the tension the Pearl Poet uses to hold you across three mornings.
