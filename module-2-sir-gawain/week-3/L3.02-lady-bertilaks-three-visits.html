📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Lady Bertilak never threatens Gawain. So how does she nearly undo the most courteous knight in the world?
She uses words. Across three morning visits she works on Gawain with praise, with reproach, and finally with a gift. This lesson names her three strategies so you can spot them in the text, and gives you the vocabulary to describe how persuasion works when it wears a smile.

📚 PART 1 · PERSUASION WITHOUT A THREAT
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Think of a time someone tried to talk you into something you did not want to do, without ever ordering you. What worked on you hardest: flattery, guilt, or a tempting offer?

The setup

Each of the three mornings, while her husband hunts, Lady Bertilak comes to Gawain's chamber and sits by his bed. She is charming, clever, and completely in control of the conversation. Gawain must stay courteous to his hostess and loyal to his host at the same time, and she keeps finding the narrow gap between the two. She is wily: she means to beguile him, to test, or assay, how far his courtesy will bend. He is in real peril, though no sword is drawn.

📚 PART 2 · HER THREE STRATEGIES
LADY BERTILAK'S PLAYBOOK · THREE WAYS TO MOVE A MAN WHO WILL NOT BE ORDERED
👏
STRATEGY 1
Flatter his reputation
She praises Gawain as the most famous, most courteous knight alive, so that refusing her feels like betraying his own good name.
⚖️
STRATEGY 2
Reproach and shame
When praise stalls, she scolds him: a knight so famed in courtesy, and yet so cold? She turns his virtue into a debt he seems to owe her.
🎁
STRATEGY 3
Offer a tempting gift
On the last day she offers tokens: first a rich ring (he refuses), then a green girdle she claims can save his life. Her largesse hides the real trap.
Notice the escalation: praise, then pressure, then a gift he is desperate to accept. She adapts each morning to what did not work the morning before.

Here is one of her sharpest lines, when she reproaches Gawain for holding back: "Ye deserve blame if ye hold not that lady who sits beside ye above all else in the world." Read how it works. She is not asking; she is judging him for not already agreeing. That is Strategy 2 in a single sentence: she makes refusal look like a failure of the very courtesy he is famous for.

📚 WORD STUDY · THIS WEEK'S FIVE WORDS
beguile — to charm or trick someone into doing something.  ·  wily — cunning, clever at getting what one wants by trickery.  ·  assay — to test or try the quality of something.  ·  peril — serious danger.  ·  largesse — generous giving, especially by someone of high rank.
Word origins: assay and essay are cousins from the same French root meaning "a trying or an attempt": to assay gold is to test it, and an essay is literally an attempt to work a question out. Largesse comes from the French for "large" or "abundant," the open-handed generosity expected of a noble. Keep these near your definition of the girdle scene: the lady's largesse is exactly how she disguises the trap.
Copia (in your Organizer): take the sentence "The lady tried to trick the knight," and rewrite it three ways: once using beguile, once using wily, and once using assay. Change the wording, not the meaning.
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND PERSUASION
The same strategies Lady Bertilak uses (flattery, reproach, a tempting offer) show up in the ads and messages an AI system can generate at scale. Learning to name a persuasive move is a defense you can use far outside this poem. Naming it is a skill; only you can practice it.
