The Exchange-of-Winnings Game

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Gawain made a friendly bargain over dinner. Why does it turn out to be the real test of his honor?
Before he ever reaches the Green Chapel, Gawain agrees to a game with his host: whatever each man wins during the day, he will hand over to the other that night. It sounds like sport. This lesson shows how that promise quietly becomes a scale that weighs Gawain's courage and his honesty at the same time.

📚 PART 1 · THE BARGAIN
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Imagine you promised a friend: "Whatever either of us earns today, we split it tonight, no matter what." Then you find something valuable and want to keep it hidden. What is the promise really testing?

What Gawain agreed to

On his first evening at the castle of Hautdesert, Gawain's host proposes a game. The lord will ride out to hunt each of the next three days. Gawain, still weary, will rest in the castle. The bargain is simple: at the end of each day, each man will give the other whatever he has won. The lord will hand over his catch from the hunt; Gawain will hand over whatever comes to him inside the walls. They swear to it, laughing, as if it were only good fun. But an oath is an oath. Gawain has bound his honor to full and honest exchange, and he does not yet know that his host's wife intends to make the "winnings" inside the castle very hard to report.

THE EXCHANGE LEDGER · THREE DAYS OF WINNINGS TRADED EACH NIGHT
Day The lord wins (the hunt) Gawain wins (the chamber) Traded honestly that night?
Day 1 A great many deer 1 kiss Yes: Gawain gives back the 1 kiss
Day 2 A fierce wild boar 2 kisses Yes: Gawain gives back the 2 kisses
Day 3 A single fox 3 kisses and a green girdle No: he gives the 3 kisses but hides the girdle
For two days the ledger balances. On the third day it does not. That one hidden row is the crack in Gawain's honor the whole poem has been building toward.
📚 PART 2 · WHY THE HIDDEN ROW MATTERS

On the third day the lady offers Gawain a green silk girdle and tells him it has a secret power: she claims that whoever wears it cannot be killed. Gawain is one day away from a blow that could take his head. He takes the girdle, and he agrees to keep it secret from her husband. That evening he gives his host the three kisses he owes. He does not mention the girdle. Notice the exact shape of the failure. Gawain does not lie outright, and he does not run from the beheading. His courage holds. What cracks is his honesty: he keeps back one winning because he wants to live. The game was built so that these two virtues would be tested by the same promise, and Gawain passes one test while quietly failing the other.

THE ONE IDEA TO HOLD
The hunt outside and the game inside are the same test in two rooms. What the lord chases in the field, the lady chases in the chamber, and the girdle is the one catch Gawain refuses to report.

📚 WORD STUDY
covenant — a solemn, binding agreement.  ·  troth — a pledge of one's faith or honor.  ·  winnings — whatever one gains or earns.
Word origins: troth is an older form of the word truth; to "plight your troth" is to pledge your truthfulness. That link is the whole point here: Gawain's troth is on the line the moment he pockets the girdle. (You will study this week's five vocabulary words in Lesson 3.02.)
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND PLOT SUMMARIES
An AI tool can list the three hunts and three kisses for you in seconds, and that can help you check a fact. It cannot decide for you whether hiding the girdle makes Gawain a coward or just a frightened, ordinary man. Weighing that is the reading work, and it stays yours.
