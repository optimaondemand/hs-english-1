🎯 TODAY'S FOCUS
How can two opposite ideas in one sentence say more than either could alone?
Gawain's story is full of contradictions: he keeps his life but loses his honor; the gift that promised safety becomes the badge of his shame. Two tools capture that tension. Antithesis sets opposites in balanced grammar; paradox states a contradiction that turns out to be true. You will learn to spot both and to write your own.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 READ IT ALOUD · NO WRONG ANSWER
Read this sentence about Gawain: "He won his life and lost his good name." Why does putting won and lost so close together hit harder than saying it plainly?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

Antithesis places two contrasting ideas in balanced, parallel grammar so the contrast rings out: "He kept the girdle, but he broke his word." The two halves are built the same way, which makes the opposition sharp. Paradox is a statement that seems to contradict itself yet reveals a truth on a second look: "The girdle that was meant to save him is the very thing that condemns him." It cannot literally save and condemn at once, and yet in the poem it does both. Antithesis is about balanced form; paradox is about a surprising truth. Writers use both to hold a complicated idea in a single line.

TELL THEM APART
Antithesis: opposites in matched grammar (a balanced form).  Paradox: a contradiction that proves true (a surprising meaning).
ON THE BOARD · ANTITHESIS ABOVE, PARADOX BELOW
ANTITHESIS · BALANCED OPPOSITES
He won his life and lost his good name.
The lady offered largesse with her hands and peril with her words.
PARADOX · A CONTRADICTION THAT IS TRUE
The girdle that was meant to save him is the thing that shames him.
To be wily, Gawain had to betray the very honesty that made him famous.
In the top pair, opposite words sit in matching slots. In the bottom pair, the sentence seems to contradict itself, and yet the poem shows it is true.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

Antithesis balances opposites; paradox holds a contradiction that turns out to be true. Both let you capture Gawain's tangled situation in one sharp line, and both will sharpen the claims in your essay.
