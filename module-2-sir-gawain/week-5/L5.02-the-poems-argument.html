The Poem’s Argument
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A poem that never states its opinion still has one. How do you find it, and how do you prove it?
The Pearl Poet never steps forward to say “Gawain is honorable” or “Gawain fails.” But the poem still argues, through what it chooses to show, repeat, and reward. Today you will learn to read a work’s implied position, and you will select the three strongest pieces of evidence for the position you will defend.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain how a text can argue a position without stating it directly.
2
I will be able to identify strong evidence and distinguish it from weak or off-topic evidence.
3
I will be able to choose my three strongest quotations or structural moments and say what each proves.
📚 PART 1 · A POEM THAT ARGUES WITHOUT SPEAKING
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
A film never says out loud “greed is bad,” yet you leave sure that is its message. Where does that message actually come from?

A poem’s implied position is the view it argues through its choices rather than its statements. To find it, read like a detective and ask three questions: What does the poem spend its most careful language on? What does it reward or punish? What does it choose to end on? The answers point to what the work values, which is its argument about honor.

Notice: finding the poem’s position is not the same as agreeing with it. Your essay can argue that the poem sides with mercy and that the poem is right, or that the poem sides with mercy and is too easy on Gawain. Either way, you first have to show what the poem itself seems to think.

📚 PART 2 · THREE STRONG PIECES OF EVIDENCE

Here are three of the strongest places to look for the poem’s position. Each is a candidate for your essay. Study how each one could support more than one thesis, depending on how you read it.

EVIDENCE MAP · THREE PLACES THE POEM SHOWS ITS HAND
EVIDENCE 1
The pentangle passage (Fitt II)
The poem gives its most careful language to Gawain’s five virtues. That care sets the standard he is measured against.
EVIDENCE 2
Bertilak’s verdict (Fitt IV)
The one character who knows the whole test judges Gawain the finest knight, faulted only “a little,” for love of his life.
EVIDENCE 3
The court adopts the girdle (ending)
The poem ends not on punishment but on shared fellowship and the motto “shame to anyone who thinks ill of it.”
↓
WHAT THE THREE TOGETHER SUGGEST
The poem holds Gawain to a perfect standard, then treats his one failure with mercy. Whether that mercy is wisdom or evasion is the argument you will make.
Find these passages in your own copy of the poem (public-domain Weston translation, 1898): the Camelot Project, University of Rochester. Copy the exact wording of any line you plan to quote.
📚 PART 3 · STRONG EVIDENCE VS. WEAK EVIDENCE

Strong evidence is specific (a quoted line or a named structural pattern), relevant (it actually bears on honor), and load-bearing (it can carry a claim, not just decorate it). Test yourself below.

THE EVIDENCE TEST
Ask of every quote: Specific? (Can I point to the exact line?)  ·  Relevant? (Does it speak to honor?)  ·  Load-bearing? (Does it prove my claim, or just sound nice?)
✎ YOUR TURN · CHOOSE THE STRONGER EVIDENCE
1. To support “the poem treats Gawain with mercy,” which is the stronger evidence?
2. To support “the poem sets a very high standard for Gawain,” which is load-bearing?
3. Which choice best shows the poem’s implied position at the close?
➡️ WHAT YOU'LL DO NEXT (IN YOUR ORGANIZER)
1
Write a 100-word brief on the poem’s position (your respondeo starter)
In about 100 words, name your three strongest pieces of evidence, quote or cite each exactly, and say in one sentence what each one proves about honor. This becomes the backbone of the “I answer that” section of your disputatio.
💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

Evidence is not a decoration you sprinkle on a finished opinion. It is the thing your opinion is built out of. Pick three passages that can each carry weight, and your argument will stand.
