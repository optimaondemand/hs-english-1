The Court’s Response: Wisdom or Evasion?
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Gawain comes home ashamed. The whole court laughs. Is that the wisest thing the court could do, or the easiest?
This is the last scene of the poem, and it is the strangest. Gawain returns to Camelot wearing the green girdle as a mark of his failure. The court responds by laughing warmly and deciding that every knight will wear a green band too. Today you will read that ending as an argument the court is making about honor, and decide whether you agree with it.

📚 PART 1 · WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENS AT THE END
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Think of a time you made a small mistake and felt terrible about it, but the people around you shrugged it off and said it was no big deal. How did their reaction feel?

At the Green Chapel, the Green Knight reveals himself as Bertilak, Gawain’s host. He explains that the whole beheading game was a test, and that Gawain passed almost all of it. Gawain flinched at the first swing and, on the third day, kept the green girdle instead of handing it over as the exchange-of-winnings game required. In the Weston translation Bertilak judges that Gawain failed “but a little,” and only because he loved his life, so he blames him the less. To Bertilak, Gawain is still the finest knight he has ever tested.

Gawain does not accept the mercy. He is furious with himself. He calls his fault cowardice and covetousness, blames the frailty of the flesh, and (in a moment worth arguing about) blames women’s wiles too. He decides to keep the girdle forever, but as a sign of shame: a badge to remind him that even the best knight can fall.

Then he rides home to Camelot. He tells the whole story honestly, expecting disgrace. Instead, the court laughs, comforts him, and makes a decision: every lord and lady of the Round Table will wear a bright green band, for love of Gawain. What Gawain wears as private shame, the court adopts as shared honor. The poem closes with its motto: Honi soit qui mal y pense, which means “shame to anyone who thinks ill of it.”

Read the ending in the public-domain Weston translation (1898): the Camelot Project, University of Rochester, or Project Gutenberg edition #14568. Use your own copy to find the exact lines you quote in your essay.

📚 PART 2 · ONE GIRDLE, TWO MEANINGS

The green girdle is the same object in everyone’s hands, but it does not mean the same thing to everyone. That gap is where your essay lives. Study the diagram: notice that Gawain and the court agree on the facts and disagree on the meaning.

THE SAME GREEN GIRDLE · READ TWO WAYS
THE GREEN GIRDLE
A woven sash. Gawain kept it hoping it would protect his life.
↓       ↓
GAWAIN READS IT AS…
A badge of shame
Proof that he failed, that even the best knight can be moved by fear. He keeps it to never forget.
THE COURT READS IT AS…
A badge of honor
A shared emblem of fellowship. They all wear a green band, turning one knight’s failing into the whole Round Table’s pride.
The poem never tells you which reading is right. That silence is your invitation to argue.
📚 PART 3 · WISDOM OR EVASION?

A strong reader can build a case for either verdict before choosing. Try both before you decide.

THE CASE FOR WISDOM
The court refuses to let one mistake define a good man. Mercy and fellowship are part of a healthy community. By all wearing the band, the knights admit that any of them might have done the same. That is humility, not denial.
THE CASE FOR EVASION
Gawain took his failing seriously; the court laughs it away. Turning a symbol of shame into a fashion may let the whole court avoid the hard question Gawain is actually asking about honor. Comfort can be a way of not looking.

2. In Bertilak’s judgment, Gawain failed mainly because he…
3. Which sentence best states the “evasion” reading of the ending?
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND THE EASY VERDICT
Ask an AI tool “Is Gawain honorable?” and it will give you a balanced, reasonable-sounding paragraph in seconds. That paragraph is a starting point, not your answer. The AI has not decided; you have to. Your essay is graded on your reasoning from the text, which is the one thing the tool cannot do for you.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

The ending is not a tidy moral. It is a disagreement, staged for you: Gawain says shame, the court says honor, and the poem leaves the last word to you. Decide which reading you can defend, because that decision is the seed of your thesis.
