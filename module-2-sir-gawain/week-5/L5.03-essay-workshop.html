📚 PART 1 · THE SHAPE OF A DISPUTATIO
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Which feels more convincing when someone argues with you?

A disputatio is the medieval scholar’s way of chasing the truth of a hard question. It does not hide from disagreement; it invites it in, states it at full strength, and then answers it. Study the structure below. You have already drafted most of these parts across Weeks 1 to 4; today you order and connect them.

THE FIVE MOVES OF A DISPUTATIO · IN ORDER
1
Quaestio (the question). Open with your alliterative catalog paragraph, then state the disputed question: Is Sir Gawain a man of honor? End the paragraph with your thesis, your direct answer.
2
Objectiones (objections). State at least two objections to your thesis, each in its strongest, fairest form. Argue against yourself here.
3
Sed contra (on the contrary). Anchor your side with one passage or structural observation from the poem that grounds your argument.
4
Respondeo (I answer that). The heart of the essay, four to six paragraphs. Reason from your three pieces of evidence to your verdict, drawing on all four Fitts.
5
Replies to objections. Return to each objection and answer it specifically and respectfully. Show why it falls short, without pretending it was never strong.
📚 PART 2 · THE VOCABULARY OF ARGUMENT

This week’s five words are the tools of a good disputatio. You will use them to talk about your argument, and to make the moves they name.

📚 WORD STUDY · WEEK 5
epitome — a perfect example of a quality; the thing that sums it up. Gawain is meant to be the epitome of the chivalric knight.
concession — admitting a point your opponent gets right, before answering it. A good reply often opens with a concession.
refutation — proving an objection wrong or incomplete. Each reply is a small refutation.
equivocation — using a word in two different senses to slide past a problem; a move to avoid. Do not let “honor” quietly change meaning mid-essay; that is equivocation.
exemplar — a model held up for imitation. The poem offers Gawain as an exemplar, then tests him.
Word origins: epitome comes from Greek roots meaning “to cut short,” a summing-up in one figure; exemplar and example share the Latin exemplum, “a sample.”
✎ COPIA PRACTICE (IN YOUR ORGANIZER)
Take the sentence “Gawain is the epitome of a knight.” Rewrite it three ways: (1) substitute a synonym for epitome; (2) alter the syntax so the sentence opens differently; (3) change the register so it sounds formal enough for your essay’s opening. Keep the meaning; change the shape.
📚 PART 3 · THE TWO HARDEST MOVES

Writing the respondeo

The respondeo is not a summary of the plot. It is a chain of reasoning. A strong pattern for each paragraph: claim (a step in your argument), evidence (a quoted or cited moment), reasoning (why that evidence proves the claim), and a link to the next step. Do this three or four times and your verdict arrives earned, not announced.

Writing a reply

A reply is a small refutation, and the best ones begin with a concession. First grant what the objection gets right; then show what it misses. That order proves you are honest and still convinces. Watch the difference:

WEAK REPLY (JUST CONTRADICTS)
“This objection is wrong. Gawain is clearly honorable and anyone who says otherwise did not read the poem.”
STRONG REPLY (CONCEDES, THEN REFUTES)
“It is true that Gawain hides the girdle, and that is a real breach of the exchange. But a single lapse under fear of death does not cancel the four virtues the pentangle names; Bertilak himself faults him only ‘a little.’”
✎ CHECK YOURSELF · PICK THE BEST ANSWER
1. Opening a reply by granting what the objection gets right is called a…
2. Letting the word “honor” mean one thing early and a different thing later is…
3. Which section is the “I answer that” core of the essay?
🤝 PART 4 · PEER-REVIEW QUESTIONS

Trade drafts with a partner (or read your own aloud as if it were a stranger’s). Answer these six questions in writing. Point to a specific line for each answer; “it’s good” is not feedback.

☐ Thesis: Underline the thesis. Does it give a clear, direct answer to the quaestio? Could someone disagree with it?
☐ Objections: Are there at least two, and is each stated fairly, at full strength? Or are they “straw” versions that are easy to knock down?
☐ Evidence: Is each major claim backed by a specific quoted or cited moment from the poem? Circle any claim with no evidence.
☐ Reasoning: After each quote, is there a sentence explaining why it proves the claim? Or does the quote just sit there?
☐ Replies: Does each reply begin with a concession and then refute? Does any reply just repeat the thesis louder?
☐ Terms: Is “honor” defined once and used consistently? Flag any equivocation.
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND REVISION
A grammar or spell checker (some are AI-powered) can flag typos and clumsy sentences, and that is a fair use. But an AI cannot decide whether your objection is really the strongest one, or whether your reply actually answers it. Those are reasoning judgments, and they are what this assignment grades. Keep the thinking yours.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

A disputatio wins by fairness, not volume. State the other side at full strength, concede what is true, and then show why your verdict still stands. That is what it means to argue honorably about honor.
