GRAMMAR 4.02
Dashes and Colons for Emphasis
Grammar Lesson
🎯 TODAY YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
Use a colon to introduce and a dash to interrupt or drive home — two marks that let you point a reader's attention exactly where you want it — and punctuate both correctly.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 READ BOTH ALOUD · NO WRONG ANSWER
A. Electra wanted one thing and it was justice.  |  B. Electra wanted one thing: justice.
The colon in B makes you pause, then delivers the word like an answer. What does that pause do that the word “and” cannot?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

A colon says to the reader, “here it comes.” It follows a complete sentence and introduces what completes or explains it: a word, a list, or a second statement. The rule to remember is that a complete sentence must come before the colon. A dash is more flexible. A single dash can drive a point home by tacking an emphatic ending onto a sentence — and a pair of dashes can interrupt a sentence to slip in extra information, the way commas do but with more force. Both marks are tools of emphasis, so both lose their power if you overuse them. A colon works best when the thing it introduces truly deserves the drumroll. A dash works best when you want a sharper break than a comma gives. Used with restraint, these marks let you control a reader's pace and steer attention to the idea that matters most, which is exactly what you want in the key sentences of an essay.

TWO PATTERNS TO REMEMBER
Complete sentence: the thing it points to.
Sentence — an emphatic add-on.
ON THE BOARD · THE MARK IN GOLD POINTS THE READER
The play ends on a single claim: the house is free.
The Chorus named her double honor: wisdom and piety.
Orestes stood beside her the whole time — alive.
The proof was undeniable — their father’s own signet ring.
The gold colon follows a full sentence and introduces what completes it. The gold dash tacks on an emphatic ending. Each mark spotlights the idea that matters most.
✎ IDENTIFY · CORRECT OR NOT?
1. Electra faced a hard choice: act alone or do nothing.
2. Chrysothemis wanted to: survive, wait, and stay safe.
3. Electra, grieving for years — refused to be silent.
➡️ NOW WRITE YOUR OWN (IN YOUR ORGANIZER)

Write two sentences about this week's reading: one that uses a colon to introduce an idea, and one that uses a dash for emphasis. You will use both moves to sharpen the key claims in your module essay.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

A colon introduces; a dash drives home. Both need a complete sentence to lean on, and both lose their power if you overuse them. Save them for the sentences you most want the reader to feel.
