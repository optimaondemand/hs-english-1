GRAMMAR 4.01
Sentence Fragments for Effect
Grammar Lesson
🎯 TODAY YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
Tell an accidental fragment from a deliberate one, and use a short intentional fragment to land a moment of emphasis, the way a playwright makes a single line hit hard.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 READ BOTH ALOUD · NO WRONG ANSWER
A. She refused to be silent, and she grieved every single day without rest.  |  B. She refused to be silent. Every day. Without rest.
Both say the same thing. Which one makes you slow down and feel the weight of it?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

A complete sentence needs a subject and a verb and expresses a full thought. A fragment is a piece of a sentence punctuated as if it were whole. Most of the time a fragment is a mistake, and you should fix it. But experienced writers sometimes use a fragment on purpose to create emphasis, rhythm, or shock. A short fragment forces a pause; it isolates an idea so the reader cannot rush past it. The key is intention and control: a deliberate fragment is short, clear, and surrounded by full sentences, so the reader can tell you meant it. If your fragments are long, frequent, or leave the reader confused, they read as errors. Use the tool sparingly. One well-placed fragment in a paragraph can carry real force; five in a row just look like broken writing. In your own essays, save the fragment for the sentence you most want the reader to remember.

ONE PATTERN TO REMEMBER
Full sentence. Short fragment for impact. Full sentence.
ON THE BOARD · THE FRAGMENT IN GOLD DOES THE WORK
Orestes was alive. Right there. And she had wept for nothing.
She would act. Alone.
He said nothing. Silence. That was his first answer.
Each gold piece is a deliberate fragment. Notice how it sits between full sentences and forces a beat of silence on the exact idea that matters.
✎ IDENTIFY · ACCIDENT OR DELIBERATE?
1. Because Chrysothemis was afraid of what might happen to them both.
2. She would act alone. No allies. No second chance.
3. Silence. That was Orestes' answer, at first.
➡️ NOW WRITE YOUR OWN (IN YOUR ORGANIZER)

Write a three-sentence moment from this week's reading (the urn scene or the recognition) that uses one deliberate fragment for emphasis, placed between two full sentences. Underline the fragment. In your essay, allow yourself no more than one such fragment: save it for the line that matters most.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

A fragment is usually an error, but one short, deliberate fragment between full sentences can carry real force. Use it once, for the idea you most want remembered.
