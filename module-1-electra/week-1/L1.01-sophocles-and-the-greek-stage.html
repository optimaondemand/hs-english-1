📚 PART 1 · THE PLAYWRIGHT AND HIS THEATRE

Think of a movie you have seen more than once. The second time, you already knew the ending. How did that change the way you watched it?

Who was Sophocles?

Sophocles (about 497–406 B.C.) lived through the height of Athens, a young democracy that had just survived invasion and was now rich, powerful, and anxious about its future. He wrote more than a hundred plays; seven survive, among them Oedipus the King, Antigone, and the play we are about to read, Electra. His plays were public events, performed once, in daylight, before thousands of citizens at a festival honoring the god Dionysus, and judged in a competition. His audience already knew the myths: they knew Agamemnon would be murdered and his children would take revenge. Sophocles was not surprising them with plot. He was asking them to watch closely as characters made terrible choices, feeling the weight of a decision the way you do on a second viewing, when you can see exactly where it leads.

📚 PART 2 · HOW A TRAGEDY IS BUILT, AND WHAT DRIVES IT

The five parts of a Greek tragedy

PrologueThe opening scene. Sets the stage and introduces the problem.
ParodosThe chorus enters, singing its first song.
EpisodesThe main scenes of action, like modern acts.
StasimonA choral ode between episodes: reflection and commentary.
ExodosThe final scene: resolution, or catastrophe.

The engine: dramatic irony

Because the audience already knew the myth, Sophocles could rely on a powerful gap: the space between what you know and what a character on stage does not. That gap is called dramatic irony. When a grieving character hopes for something you know will not come, or fears something you know is already solved, every line lands twice: once as they mean it, and once as you hear it, knowing more. Watch for it from the very first scene of Electra.

📚 WORD STUDY
arete — excellence, living up to one's full potential.  ·  kleos — glory earned through great deeds.  ·  xenia — hospitality, a sacred duty.  ·  hubris — destructive pride that overreaches.
Word origins: hubris came straight into English unchanged; we still call a proud overreacher's fall an act of hubris. Copia (in your Organizer): take the sentence "The hero sought glory in battle," and rewrite it twice: once so "glory" clearly means kleos, and once so the hero's ambition becomes hubris.
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND FIRST IMPRESSIONS
An AI tool can hand you a tidy summary of "Greek tragedy" in seconds, which can be a fine way to check a fact. It cannot catch the exact moment in Electra where dramatic irony makes your stomach drop. That noticing is the work, and it stays yours.
