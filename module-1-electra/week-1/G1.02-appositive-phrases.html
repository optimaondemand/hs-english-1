🎯 TODAY'S FOCUS
How can you fold two facts about a character into one smooth sentence, without adding a single new idea?
You will recognize an appositive phrase, punctuate it with commas, and use one to pack more information into a sentence smoothly. It is a move you will use to introduce evidence in your essay.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 READ BOTH ALOUD · NO WRONG ANSWER
A. Clytaemnestra killed Agamemnon. Clytaemnestra was his wife.   |   B. Clytaemnestra, his wife, killed Agamemnon.
Version B folds two facts into one smooth sentence. How did it do that?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

An appositive is a noun or noun phrase placed right beside another noun to rename or explain it. In "Clytaemnestra, his wife, killed Agamemnon," the phrase his wife is an appositive renaming Clytaemnestra. Appositives let a writer add identifying detail without starting a new sentence; they are one of the most useful tools you have for writing with economy and grace. When the appositive simply adds extra information (information the sentence would survive without), set it off with commas: Orestes, Electra's brother, returns in secret. You can drop "Electra's brother" and the sentence still stands, so it takes commas. Appositives can be short (a word) or long (a whole phrase), and skilled writers use them to define a term the moment they introduce it.

ONE PATTERN TO REMEMBER
Noun, renaming phrase, … rest of sentence.  A comma on each side of the extra information.
ON THE BOARD · SPOT THE APPOSITIVE
Clytaemnestra, his wife, killed Agamemnon.
Orestes, Electra’s brother, returns in secret.
Aegisthus, the usurper, sits on the throne.
The gold underline marks the appositive: a phrase that renames the noun just before it. Notice a comma sits on each side of it.

✎ IDENTIFY & PRACTICE
In your notebook, find the appositive in each sentence (the phrase that renames the noun before it). Then open the check.
1. Sophocles, a playwright of Athens, wrote Electra.
2. Electra, the grieving daughter, refuses to be silent.
3. The Tutor, an old and trusted servant, guides Orestes home.
4. Mycenae, a city rich in gold, is the setting of the play.
5. Justice, the debt we owe one another, drives every character.
▶  Try all five in your notebook first, then check your answers
1. a playwright of Athens · 2. the grieving daughter · 3. an old and trusted servant · 4. a city rich in gold · 5. the debt we owe one another. Each renames the noun just before it, and each is set off by commas.
➡️ NOW WRITE YOUR OWN (IN YOUR ORGANIZER)

Write three sentences about characters in Electra, each using an appositive to introduce and define the character (like sentences 2 and 3 above). Remember the comma on each side. You will reuse this move to introduce evidence in your essay.

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

An appositive renames a noun right where it stands, and extra information gets a comma on each side. It is how you introduce a character or a term in one clean sentence instead of two.
