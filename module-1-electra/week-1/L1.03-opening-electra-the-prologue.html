📚 PART 1 · THE PLAN, AND THE ADVANTAGE IT HANDS YOU
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Picture standing in a room where you know a piece of news that would change everything for the person across from you, and they do not know it yet. What does it feel like to hold that knowledge?

What happens in the Prologue

The play opens at dawn outside the palace at Mycenae. An old servant, the Tutor who saved Orestes as a child, brings the grown Orestes home to avenge his murdered father, Agamemnon. The god Apollo, through the oracle at Delphi, has told Orestes to take his vengeance not by open force but by stealth. So they settle on a plan: they will spread a false report that Orestes has died in a chariot race, and Orestes will return in disguise carrying an urn said to hold his own ashes. It is a cold, clever plan, and it hands you, the reader, a terrible advantage. Because at that very moment, from inside the house, we hear Electra crying out in grief for a brother she believes is far away and may be dead. She does not know he is standing yards from her, alive. You do. Every word she speaks from here on you will hear twice: once as she means it, and once knowing what she does not. That is dramatic irony, and Sophocles has just switched it on.

DRAMATIC IRONY · THE GAP BETWEEN WHAT YOU KNOW AND WHAT ELECTRA KNOWS
👁️ WHAT YOU (THE READER) KNOW
Orestes is alive. He is home in disguise. The report of his death is false, part of the plan.
😢 WHAT ELECTRA KNOWS
Her brother is far away, and may be dead. She grieves, alone, for a loss that has not happened.
THE GAP = DRAMATIC IRONY. Every line she speaks lands twice.
THE FIRST WORDS OF THE PLAY
“Son of him who led our hosts at Troy of old, son of Agamemnon… deem that thou seest Mycenae rich in gold.”
The Tutor, to Orestes (trans. Jebb). The very first line names the dead father whose murder sets everything in motion.
📚 PART 2 · READ, THEN ANNOTATE
Read the opening of Electra, from the Tutor's first speech (the play's first lines) through Electra's first lament, the point where she enters crying out to the light and the air. Read it aloud if you can. These lines were written to be heard.
🔗 Open Electra (public-domain text, trans. R. C. Jebb)
Free, full text at the Internet Classics Archive. Bookmark it: you will read from this same translation all module. Start at the very top and stop when Electra finishes her first burst of grief and the Chorus is about to arrive.
✏ ANNOTATE AS YOU READ
Mark up your own copy: a note in the margin, a highlight, or a comment in your Organizer. As you read, flag every place you find:
▶
an expression of grief (Electra's words are full of them), and
▶
a moment of dramatic irony: a line that means one thing to Electra and another to you, because you know Orestes lives.
✎ CHECK YOURSELF · PICK THE BEST ANSWER
1. What is Orestes' plan for taking vengeance?
2. Where exactly is the dramatic irony in this opening?
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND THE READING ITSELF
It is easy to ask AI for "the plot of Electra" and skip the reading. A summary hands you the facts but not the experience of knowing what Electra does not, and that experience is the lesson. Read the Prologue yourself first. If afterward you want to confirm you understood Orestes' plan, that is a fair check.
➡️ WHAT YOU'LL DO NEXT · SPEAK YOUR THINKING (record 1–3 minutes)
Record yourself, using any tool you like (your phone, the recorder in Canvas, or your VR space), answering these in order:
1
What do we learn about Electra's situation?
2

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

From the first scene, you know more than Electra does. Hold that gap as you read. Her grief will hit you harder than it hits her, precisely because you can see what she cannot.
