Can grief become an act, and can an act of mourning be an act of justice?

📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

In Greek tragedy a dream is rarely just a dream. The Greeks treated dreams and omens as messages that could carry the will of the gods, so a frightening dream in a play is often a signal that a reckoning is on its way. Chrysothemis brings word of one: Clytemnestra dreamed that the murdered Agamemnon returned, planted his scepter at the hearth, and from it a branch grew up and shaded all of Mycenae. To Electra the meaning is plain: the dead father's line will rise again and cover the land, and the gods are stirring on the side of justice. Watch what this news does to her. She does not simply feel comforted; she acts. She tells Chrysothemis to throw away the gifts their mother is sending to the tomb, because offerings from the woman who killed him are an insult, not an honor, and to lay down instead a lock of their own hair and a plain girdle: poor gifts, but honest ones. This is the week's quiet turning point. Electra's grief, which has looked like pure suffering, becomes a form of resistance. Honoring the dead correctly is itself a claim about justice: a refusal to let the murderers decide what the father is owed.

📖 WEEKLY WORD STUDY · WEEK 2
Five words from this week's reading, defined in context. Add them to your Organizer's vocabulary page. You will need at least three of them in this week's Evidence Log and your module essay.
libation (noun) — a pouring out of liquid, or an offering left, in honor of the dead or the gods. Electra rejects her mother's libation at the tomb.
omen (noun) — a sign believed to reveal what is coming, good or ill. The dream is read as an omen that justice is near.
prudence (noun) — careful good sense in judging how to act; caution guided by foresight. Chrysothemis calls her submission prudence; Electra calls it fear.
requite (verb) — to pay back, to return like for like, whether a kindness or a wrong. Electra means to see her father's murder requited.
lament (noun / verb) — an open expression of grief; to mourn aloud. Chrysothemis begs her sister to end her daily lament.
🕐 WHERE THE WORD COMES FROM
Prudence comes from the Latin prudentia, a worn-down form of providentia: pro- ("ahead") plus videre ("to see"). At its root, prudence means seeing ahead. That buried image is exactly Chrysothemis's argument: she claims to be looking down the road at the danger Electra ignores. When you meet the word providence or provide, listen for the same "seeing ahead."
