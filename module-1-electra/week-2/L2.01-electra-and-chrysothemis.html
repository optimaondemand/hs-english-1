Two Sisters, Two Answers to Injustice
Reading Lesson · Electra meets Chrysothemis
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
When two people share the same wrong, can they answer it in opposite ways and both be right?
Today you read the Electra–Chrysothemis scene: the first meeting of the two sisters, where Chrysothemis, richly dressed, urges Electra to stop her open mourning and bow to the rulers, and Electra refuses. Two women share the same wrong and choose opposite responses to it. Along the way you will sharpen a reader's tool: telling a simile from a metaphor, and asking what each comparison is really doing.

📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Imagine you and a sibling both know something in your home is wrong. One of you says so, loudly, every day. The other stays quiet to keep the peace. Neither approves of the wrong. Who is acting more rightly?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

Sophocles gives Electra a sister, Chrysothemis, so that we can watch two people carry the same grief and reach opposite conclusions about what justice requires. Chrysothemis agrees, privately, that their father was wronged, but she has decided that open protest is useless when you have no power to act on it, and so she keeps her comforts and her safety. Electra has decided the opposite: to say the truth aloud whatever it costs, because to fall silent would be to join the wrong. As they argue, notice how each sister reaches for comparison to make her point land. A simile compares two things using like or as ("she wept like a bird robbed of its young"), keeping the two things side by side so you feel the likeness. A metaphor goes further and says one thing simply is another ("her grief is a fire that will not go out"), fusing them so the image becomes the thing itself. Both are tools for making an abstract feeling, such as grief, fear, or defiance, into something you can see. When you find one in this scene, do not stop at naming it. Ask what it lets the speaker say that plain statement could not, and whose side it quietly asks you to take.

TWO SISTERS, ONE INJUSTICE · SAME WRONG, OPPOSITE ANSWERS
ELECTRA
HER CHOICE
Open, daily mourning; refuse to be silent.
HER REASONING
To fall silent before a murder is to join the wrong.
WHAT SHE RISKS
Her safety and comfort; the rulers' anger.
CHRYSOTHEMIS
HER CHOICE
Quiet submission; bow to the rulers.
HER REASONING
Protest is useless without the power to act on it.
WHAT SHE RISKS
Her conscience; the charge of cowardice.
Both begin from the same fact: their father was wronged. Everything after that is a choice. As you read, decide which choice you find more just, and why.

CHRYSOTHEMIS DEFENDS HER CAUTION
"I care not to seem active, without the power to hurt."
— Chrysothemis (trans. Jebb). She will not make a show of protest she cannot back with force. Electra answers: "Do not teach me to betray my friends."
✏️ ANNOTATE AS YOU READ
On your own copy, mark up the scene. As you read, flag every place you find:
▶
a simile (a comparison with like or as), and label it S;
▶
a metaphor (one thing said to be another), and label it M;
▶
every reference to justice, and each place Electra accuses Chrysothemis of something. Keep this thread going all module.

⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · AI AND WHAT A COMPARISON DOES
AI can label a line as a simile or a metaphor quickly, and usually correctly. What it cannot do is tell you what that comparison is doing here: whether it makes Electra's grief look noble or excessive, and whose side it nudges you toward. That reading is the actual work of this lesson, and it stays yours.
🎤 SPEAK YOUR THINKING (RECORD 2–4 MINUTES)
Record yourself, using any tool you like (your phone, the recorder in Canvas, or your VR space), answering these in order:
1. What is Chrysothemis's reasoning for bowing to the rulers, and how is her experience of the family's injustice different from Electra's?
2. How does Electra respond? Be specific about how she contrasts her sister's words with her deeds.
3. How does this scene add to the play's unfolding question of what justice is and who owes it?

Two sisters, one wrong, opposite answers. Sophocles refuses to hand you the winner. Keep the contrast close: it is the question your whole module essay on justice will answer.
