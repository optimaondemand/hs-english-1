Grammar Lesson
🎯 TODAY YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
Recognize a periodic sentence (one that holds its main point until the end), explain the suspense it creates, and write one of your own about the play.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 READ BOTH ALOUD · NO WRONG ANSWER
A. Clytaemnestra felt relief when she heard the report.   |   B. When she heard the report, when she was sure the threat was gone, Clytaemnestra felt relief.
Both are correct. In which one do you have to wait for the main point?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

A periodic sentence saves its main clause (its central idea) for the end, placing modifiers, subordinate clauses, and phrases first. The reader must hold everything in suspense until the final words complete the thought. Its opposite is the loose sentence, which states the main idea first and then adds detail: Electra despaired, believing her brother dead, seeing no path to justice. The same content can be built either way. Turn it around and it becomes periodic: Believing her brother dead, seeing no path to justice, Electra despaired. Notice how the periodic version withholds the word "despaired" until the last beat, so the whole sentence leans toward it. Writers reach for periodic sentences when they want to build pressure toward a climactic word, which is exactly why they suit a tragedy: the shape of the sentence imitates the suspense of the plot. Punctuation follows the rules you already know: introductory phrases and clauses take a comma before the main clause arrives.

ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
Modifiers first → main clause last = periodic (suspense). Introductory phrases take a comma before the main clause lands.
ON THE BOARD · PERIODIC SENTENCES · THE MAIN POINT ARRIVES LAST
Because the oracle commanded stealth, Orestes spread a false report.
Cheated of her home, robbed of her father, and now told her brother is dead, Electra still refuses to be silent.
When the debate ended, when neither woman would yield, the Chorus spoke.
The gold main clause is held to the end. Everything before it makes you wait, and the sentence leans toward its final words.
✎ YOUR TURN · PERIODIC OR LOOSE?
1. Hearing the Tutor describe the crash, and believing every detail, Clytaemnestra felt safe at last.
2. Electra wept for her brother, sure that all hope was gone.
✎ MORE PRACTICE (IN YOUR NOTEBOOK FIRST)
Decide whether each sentence is periodic (main point last) or loose (main point first). Write your answers down before you open the check.
1. Because the oracle commanded stealth, Orestes chose to spread a false report.
2. Orestes chose stealth, following the oracle's command and trusting Apollo.
3. When the messenger finished, when the last detail had landed, the queen smiled.
▶  Write your three answers, then check
1. Periodic: the main clause ("Orestes chose…") arrives after the reason.
2. Loose: the main point comes first, then the detail follows.
3. Periodic: the reader waits through two clauses for "the queen smiled."
