📖 WHAT YOU ARE READING TODAY
Is a strange dream a sign of hope, or a warning of more sorrow to come?
The Chorus's long response to Clytaemnestra's dream, which Chrysothemis has just reported. For nearly two pages the Chorus stops mediating between the sisters and speaks its own mind about the crisis: who is guilty, where the trouble began, and whether the dream points toward hope or despair.

📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 NO WRONG ANSWER
Think of a time someone told you about a strange dream and then asked, "What do you think it means?" Reading a dream is really an argument: the same images can point toward good news or bad. What did you find yourself doing?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

In a Greek tragedy the Chorus is not a background crowd; it is the play thinking out loud. Here, after Chrysothemis describes the dream, the Chorus delivers a full ode weighing what it means. Read closely and you will see the Chorus lining up on Electra's side of the question. It hears in the dream "the voice of Justice," and it looks forward to the Avenger: a force, human or divine, that will come to set the killing right. It then reaches back into family history, naming Pelops and his charioteer Myrtilus, whose betrayal and drowning began the curse: "since Myrtilus sank to rest beneath the waves… this house was never yet free from misery." That backward glance matters. The Chorus is saying the crisis did not start with Clytaemnestra; it started generations ago, and each wrong has demanded another. For Electra, the dream is therefore a flicker of hope: if Justice really is stirring, her long grief is not pointless. But hope and dread sit close together here, because the same Justice that might avenge her father is the Justice that has been drowning this family in blood for generations.

HOW THE CHORUS READS THE DREAM · THE SAME OMEN, WEIGHED TWO WAYS
▲ TOWARD HOPE
"The voice of Justice"
The dream is a sign that Justice is stirring and the Avenger is coming. Electra's long grief has not been for nothing. The wrong done to Agamemnon will be repaid.
⚖
the dream
▼ TOWARD DESPAIR
"Never yet free from misery"
The same Justice has been drowning this house in blood since Pelops betrayed Myrtilus. If the curse still runs, more sorrow is coming, not peace.
The Chorus tips the scale toward hope, but it never lets go of the dread. In this house, the arrival of Justice has always meant another death.

THE CHORUS ON JUSTICE
“Justice, that hath sent the presage, will come, triumphant in her righteous strength.”
— the Chorus (trans. Jebb). A "presage" is an omen or sign: here, the dream itself.
📚 WORD STUDY — avenger / vengeance
The Chorus longs for the Avenger. In context, an avenger is one who inflicts punishment on behalf of a wrong done to another; vengeance is that punishment carried out as a repayment for injury.
🕐 ETYMOLOGY
Avenge comes through Old French from Latin vindicare, "to claim, to set free, to punish." The same root gives us vindicate (to clear of blame) and vindictive (bent on revenge). Notice how one Latin root can branch toward justice done rightly (vindicate) or toward revenge that will not let go (vindictive): exactly the tension this play keeps alive.
Copia (write it several ways). In your Organizer, express this one idea, Electra waits for the wrong to be repaid, in four different sentences, each using a different member of the word family: avenge, avenger, vengeance, vindicate. Vary your sentence openings, not just the words.

⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · WORKING WITH AI
The names Pelops and Myrtilus may be new to you, and AI can quickly give you their myth. That is a fair way to fill a background gap. What AI cannot do for you is judge why the Chorus reaches back to them right here: how naming the family's oldest crime shapes its reading of the dream. That connection is the work of this lesson, and it stays yours.
