📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 READ BOTH ALOUD · NO WRONG ANSWER
A. Electra held the urn. She wept over her brother's ashes.   |   B. Holding the urn, Electra wept over her brother's ashes.
Both are correct. What did version B do to the two sentences?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

A participle is a verb form used as an adjective. Present participles end in -ing (weeping, waiting, hearing); past participles usually end in -ed, -en, or an irregular form (cheated, driven, torn). A participial phrase is a participle plus the words that go with it, and the whole phrase acts like one adjective describing a noun: in Holding the urn, Electra wept, the phrase Holding the urn describes Electra. These phrases let you combine ideas gracefully and show what a character is doing while something else happens. Two punctuation habits will carry you far. First, when a participial phrase opens a sentence, set it off with a comma: Believing the report, Clytaemnestra rejoiced. Second, and this is the trap, the phrase must sit next to the noun it actually describes, or it "dangles." Believing the report, the news brought relief is wrong, because the news did not believe anything. Keep the doer of the participle right beside it, and the phrase will point where you mean.

ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
Opening participial phrase → comma → the noun it describes.
Disguised as a stranger, the Tutor told his false story. (The Tutor is disguised, so the phrase points to the right noun.)
ON THE BOARD · PARTICIPIAL PHRASES · EACH DESCRIBES A NOUN
Grieving for her father, Electra refuses her mother's rule.
Driven by love for Aegisthus, Clytaemnestra struck down her husband.
The Chorus, moved by Electra's suffering, offers her comfort.
Thrown from his chariot, the runner in the false report is said to be crushed.
The gold participial phrase sits right beside the noun it describes. Present participles end in -ing (grieving, moved is past); the last two use past participles (driven, thrown).

💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

A participial phrase is one describing phrase built on a verb. It combines ideas gracefully, but only if it sits right beside the noun it describes. Keep the doer next to the phrase and you will never dangle a modifier.
