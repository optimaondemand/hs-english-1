Reading Lesson · Lay out both sides, then weigh them
📖 WHAT YOU ARE READING TODAY
When both sides claim justice, how does a careful reader decide which one holds up?
The play's central debate: Clytaemnestra comes out of the palace, and she and Electra argue face to face about whether killing Agamemnon was just. This is the scene the whole play has been building toward. Your job today is a specific skill: to lay out both arguments fairly and then weigh which one holds up.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to state each woman's claim about the killing of Agamemnon in a single fair sentence.
2
I will be able to line up the evidence each uses, and show how each answers the other: both hinge on Iphigenia.
3
I will be able to judge which argument is more valid, and defend that judgment with evidence.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 NO WRONG ANSWER
Think of a dispute where both sides believe they are in the right. Try, in your head, to state the side you disagree with fairly, in one honest sentence. How did that feel?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

When two people argue and each claims justice, a careful reader does not just pick a favorite. A careful reader first lays out both arguments in full: the claim, the reasons, and the evidence, and only then decides which one is stronger. That is what today asks of you. Both Clytaemnestra and Electra rest their entire case on a single event from the past: Agamemnon's sacrifice of his daughter Iphigenia to the goddess Artemis so the Greek fleet could sail for Troy. They read that one act in opposite ways. Clytaemnestra says it makes Agamemnon a murderer of his own child, so killing him was justice; she even claims, "I had an ally: Justice." Electra answers that Agamemnon acted under compulsion, because Artemis herself held the fleet and demanded it, so the sacrifice was not a free crime; and she charges that her mother's real motive was not justice at all but her love for Aegisthus. The two claims cannot both be fully right, and the play does not simply hand you the answer. Your task is to hold both up honestly and then weigh them: whose evidence is more solid, and whose reasoning has the weaker link?

⚖ THE HINGE · BOTH ARGUMENTS TURN ON ONE EVENT
The sacrifice of Iphigenia
Clytaemnestra reads it as a free murder that earned death. Electra reads it as a forced act commanded by a god. Decide how to read that one event, and you have largely decided the debate.

✅ WEIGH THE TWO
Answer each on your own first. These test whether you can hold both arguments fairly and judge their reasoning, not just pick a favorite.
✎ PICK THE BEST ANSWER
1. What single past event do both arguments turn on?
2. Which is the stronger way Electra weakens her mother's claim to justice?
3. What does it mean to judge the "validity" of an argument?
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · WORKING WITH AI
If you ask AI "who is right, Clytaemnestra or Electra?" it will hand you a verdict. But a verdict handed to you is not an argument you can defend. The skill here is weighing: showing which reasons are strong and which are weak, in your own words, from the text. AI can summarize a position; deciding which position is more valid, and why, is the thinking you are graded on.
✎ WRITE IT OUT (1–2 paragraphs in your Organizer)
Both women appeal to the gods (mainly Phoebus / Apollo), to the law of the city of Mycenae, and to the family (marriage, and the bond of parent and child). Each believes she has kept her duty to all three. In your Organizer: (1) summarize both arguments; (2) identify how each woman refutes the other; and (3) explain which argument you find more valid, and why. Point to specific lines.
