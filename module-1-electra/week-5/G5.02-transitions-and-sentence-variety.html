GRAMMAR 5.02
Transitions and Sentence Variety
Grammar Lesson · Week 5
🎯 TODAY'S FOCUS
Why does one paragraph read like reasoning and another like a list of facts?
You will choose transitions that name the real relationship between your ideas, and vary how your sentences begin and how long they run, so your essay reads like an argument moving forward, not a list.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 READ BOTH ALOUD · NO WRONG ANSWER
A. Electra wants revenge. Chrysothemis wants safety. They argue. Electra decides to act alone.
B. Electra wants revenge, but Chrysothemis wants safety. Because they cannot agree, Electra decides to act alone.
Both say the same facts. Why does B feel like reasoning and A like a list?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

Two habits make writing read as connected thinking rather than a pile of facts: transitions and sentence variety. A transition is a word or phrase that names the relationship between two ideas, and the relationship has to be true. Use because, since, and therefore for cause; however, but, and although for contrast; for example and in particular for evidence; finally and in the end for sequence. A wrong transition is worse than none, because it promises a relationship the sentences do not deliver. Sentence variety is the second habit. When every sentence is the same length and starts the same way, usually with the subject, a paragraph turns flat and readers stop noticing it. Fix this two ways. Vary your openings by starting some sentences with a subordinate clause or a transition (Although the plan succeeds…) instead of the subject. Vary your lengths by placing a short, direct sentence after a long one; the short sentence lands hard because of the contrast. You already know how to build complex sentences from earlier this module, so here you use that skill on purpose, mixing simple and complex sentences so the important ideas stand out. Read your draft aloud one last time: the places where you stumble are usually the places that need a transition or a change of rhythm.

ON THE BOARD · SPOT THE TRANSITION
Electra grieves for years; however, her mother celebrates the death.
Orestes must act by stealth because the oracle commanded it.
The revenge succeeds. In the end, its cost is left unresolved.
The gold words are the transitions. Each one names a real relationship: contrast, then cause, then sequence.
TRANSITIONS BY THE JOB THEY DO
Cause / effectbecause, since, therefore, as a result
Contrasthowever, but, although, on the other hand
Evidencefor example, for instance, in particular
Sequence / closefirst, then, finally, in the end
✎ IDENTIFY & PRACTICE
Choose the transition that names the true relationship in each sentence.
✎ PICK THE TRANSITION THAT FITS
1. Electra grieves for years; ______ her mother celebrates the death.
2. Orestes must act by stealth ______ the oracle commanded it.
3. The revenge succeeds. ______, the cost of it is left unresolved.
4. Chrysothemis offers a sound argument; ______ Electra refuses to listen.
➡️ NOW WRITE YOUR OWN (IN YOUR ORGANIZER)
Reread one paragraph of your essay draft. Add or fix one transition so it names a real relationship, and rewrite one sentence so it begins differently from the sentence before it. Then place a short sentence right after your longest one and notice the effect.
💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

Transitions name how your ideas relate; varied sentences keep a reader awake. Use both on purpose, and your paragraphs read like an argument moving forward.
