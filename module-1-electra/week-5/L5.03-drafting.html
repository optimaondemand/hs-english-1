📚 PART 1 · WEAVING IN EVIDENCE
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Open your outline and look at the first quotation you chose. Before you paste it, what will your run-up sentence do first?

A draft is where your outline becomes prose, and the skill that most separates strong essays from weak ones is how the writer handles evidence. A quotation should never be dropped into a paragraph on its own line, orphaned; it should be woven into a sentence you write. The reliable pattern is introduce, quote, explain. First you set up the line in your own words, naming the speaker and the moment. Then you give the quotation, exact and inside quotation marks, cited by its place in the play rather than by a page number from any printed edition. Then, in the sentences that follow, you explain what the line shows and how it advances your claim: this is the reasoning, and it should be longer than the quote itself. Keep quotations short. A phrase or a line usually proves more than a long block, because you can point precisely at the words that matter. As you draft, reach for the module’s vocabulary of justice on purpose. Words like justice, duty, piety, vengeance, and polis are not decoration; each one names an idea the play tests, and using the exact word lets you say in one term what would otherwise take a clumsy phrase. Do not stop to polish every sentence now. The goal today is a complete draft, beginning to end, that you can improve tomorrow.

INTRODUCE · QUOTE · EXPLAIN
When Chrysothemis pleads with her sister to give up the plan, she cautions that “Justice is sometimes dangerous.” The warning lands because it comes from the sister who has been right about survival all along, and it forces the audience to weigh the rightness of the cause against the cost of pursuing it.
1. introduce (your words)   2. quote (exact, brief)   3. explain (the longest part)
📚 PART 2 · CITE AND NAME PRECISELY
CITING THE TEXT
We read the free, public-domain Jebb translation. Cite by speaker and moment (for example, “in Clytaemnestra’s defense of the killing”), not by a page number from a printed book. The full text lives here: classics.mit.edu/Sophocles/electra.html.
➡️ USE THE WORDS ON PURPOSE
Your essay must use at least three Word Study terms from the module accurately, for example justice, duty, piety, vengeance, polis, or anagnorisis. As you draft, underline each one you use so you can confirm you have met the requirement.
✎ CHECK YOURSELF · PICK THE BEST ANSWER
1. What are the three steps for using a quotation?
2. How should you cite a line from our translation?
⚠️ A NOTE ON AI
An AI tool can define a term like anagnorisis and can suggest a smoother way to phrase a sentence. It cannot verify that a quotation is really in the play, and it sometimes invents lines that sound right but were never written. Every quotation in your essay must be one you found in the actual text and can point to.
💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

A quotation proves nothing on its own. Introduce it, keep it short and exact, and spend your longest sentences explaining what it shows. Get a full draft down today; you will sharpen it tomorrow.
