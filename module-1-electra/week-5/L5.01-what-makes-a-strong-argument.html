What Makes a Strong Argument

📖 WHAT YOU ARE LEARNING TODAY
You have read the whole play and asked the same question five times over: what is justice, and what is owed to gods, city, and family? This week you turn that thinking into an essay. Today you learn what an argument is made of: a claim, its evidence, and the reasoning that ties the two together.

1
I will be able to name the three parts of an argument and tell them apart.
2
I will be able to judge whether a piece of writing states a real position or only summarizes the plot.
📚 PART 1 · FROM A FEELING TO A CLAIM
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Finish this sentence about the end of the play: “The revenge Electra and Orestes take is ______.” Which word fits your reading right now?

An argument is not the same as a summary. A summary tells a reader what happened; an argument tells a reader what you think is true and why they should think so too. Every strong argument has three parts, and they always work in the same order. First comes the claim: a position someone could reasonably disagree with. “Electra suffers” is not a claim, because no one disputes it. “Sophocles makes Electra’s revenge feel just even though it costs her everything” is a claim, because a thoughtful reader could push back. Second comes the evidence: the specific proof you point to, usually a line or moment from the text. Evidence is not decoration; it is the ground your claim stands on, so it must be exact. Third, and most often skipped, comes the reasoning: the sentences where you explain how the evidence supports the claim. Evidence never speaks for itself. A quotation dropped into a paragraph proves nothing until you say what it shows and why it matters. When Clytaemnestra insists she killed Agamemnon with “Justice” as her ally, that line is evidence; but only your reasoning can turn it into a point about how Sophocles lets both mother and daughter claim the same word. Claim, evidence, reasoning: a paragraph that has all three persuades; a paragraph missing any one of them does not.

THE ANATOMY OF AN ARGUMENT
CLAIM
A position a reasonable person could dispute. Your answer to the question.
→
EVIDENCE
An exact line or moment from the text that supports the claim.
→
REASONING
Your explanation of how the evidence proves the claim. The part readers remember.
Read left to right: a claim needs evidence, and evidence is only worth something once your reasoning explains it.
📚 PART 2 · THE THREE PARTS AT WORK

A model paragraph (watch the three parts)

Sophocles refuses to let revenge feel like a clean victory. Even as the killing succeeds, Chrysothemis warns her sister that “Justice is sometimes dangerous,” and the play never proves her wrong. That warning matters because it plants doubt at the exact moment Electra is most certain: the audience is asked to celebrate a just outcome and to fear its cost at the same time, so the ending reads as both deliverance and warning rather than simple triumph.

⚠️ A NOTE ON AI
An AI tool can list the parts of an argument and can even name a claim in a passage. It cannot decide what you believe about the justice of Electra’s revenge, and it cannot supply the reasoning that connects a line from the play to your position. That connection is the thinking your essay is graded on, and it has to be yours.
💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

A summary reports; an argument persuades. Keep claim, evidence, and reasoning together in every paragraph you write this week, and your essay will do more than retell the play.
