📚 PART 1 · SETTLE YOUR POSITION
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Reread your four Quick Writes. Finish this line in your head, then pick the shape it takes: “By the end of the play, I think justice in Electra is ______.”

A thesis is a claim with the stakes made clear. The module question asks how Electra dramatizes what is owed to gods, city, and family, and whether the revenge that ends the play is just. Your job is not to summarize the answer the play gives, since the play deliberately refuses a tidy one, but to argue for the reading you find most convincing and to defend it with the text. Start by settling your position in a single sentence you could defend to someone who disagrees. Then work backward: what in the play made you believe it? A strong case usually rests on two or three distinct moments rather than one, and the strongest cases reach for evidence from different points in the drama: Clytaemnestra’s defense of her own act, Chrysothemis’s warnings, the Chorus’s verdict, the recognition scene, the final claim that “freedom is won.” Group your evidence before you write. Each group becomes one body paragraph, and each paragraph needs its own smaller claim that supports the thesis, its own line from the text, and its own reasoning. An outline is where you check that the whole case holds together before you spend your energy on sentences. If you cannot say in your outline how a quotation proves your point, that quotation is not yet evidence; it is just a line you liked.

📝 THREE POSITIONS YOU MIGHT DEFEND
These are starting points, not the only options. Pick one, or shape your own.
Sophocles presents the revenge as just: a debt owed to a murdered father and finally paid.
Sophocles shows the revenge as a new injustice, a cycle of blood that freedom cannot end.
Sophocles leaves it deliberately unresolved, so that justice and cost are felt together.
