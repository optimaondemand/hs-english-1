LESSON 5.04
Revising and Presenting
Lesson · Week 5 (Project Week)
📖 WHAT YOU ARE LEARNING TODAY
A first draft is not a finished essay. Today you revise yours using feedback and a checklist, then prepare a short, optional spoken presentation of your thesis. Revising is where good writers do most of their real work.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to revise a draft in response to feedback, strengthening claim, evidence, and reasoning.
2
I will be able to present my thesis aloud clearly and support it with one piece of evidence.
📚 PART 1 · REVISE, THEN PROOFREAD
💭 WARM-UP · NO WRONG ANSWER
Read only your first and last sentences out loud. Do they name the same position?

Revising is not the same as proofreading. Proofreading fixes surface errors: spelling, a missing comma. Revising asks the harder question: does the argument actually work? Start by reading your draft as if you disagreed with it, hunting for the weakest paragraph. Feedback helps here, because a reader notices gaps you cannot: a claim that turned out to be a summary, a quotation left unexplained, a paragraph whose smaller claim never connected back to the thesis. When you receive feedback, sort it before you act. Some notes are about the argument (a body paragraph needs stronger reasoning) and some are about mechanics (a run-on sentence); handle the argument first, because there is no point polishing a sentence you may cut. Move, add, and delete whole sentences freely; that is what revision means. Only when the case is sound do you turn to conventions: sentence variety, transitions, and a final proofread. The last stage of the week is optional but worth doing: preparing to say your thesis aloud. Speaking an argument forces a clarity that hiding behind a page does not. If you cannot state your position and one reason for it in twenty seconds of plain speech, the written version is probably not clear yet either.

A REVISION LOG · TURN EACH NOTE INTO A CHANGE
📝 WHAT THE NOTE SAID ✎ WHAT I CHANGED
“Your second paragraph just retells the scene.” Added two sentences of reasoning that explain how the line proves my claim.
“This quotation appears with no set-up.” Wrote a run-up sentence naming Chrysothemis and the moment before the quote.
“Your conclusion says something new.” Rewrote the last paragraph to restate my actual position in fresh words.
Keep your own version of this log in your Organizer: for each comment, record what the note said and what you changed. If you chose not to change something, say why.
📚 PART 2 · CHECK, THEN SPEAK
✅ REVISION CHECKLIST
☐ My thesis states a position a reader could dispute.
☐ Every body paragraph has a claim, evidence, and reasoning.
☐ Each quotation is introduced and then explained; none stands alone.
☐ I used at least three Word Study terms accurately.
☐ My conclusion matches the position I ended up defending.
☐ I read it aloud once and fixed what sounded wrong.
🎤 OPTIONAL · PRESENT YOUR THESIS ALOUD (about 60 seconds)
Prepare a short spoken version of your argument and, if you choose, record it on any device you have: a phone, the Canvas media tool, or a VR space. Say it in three moves:
1. State the question and your thesis in one clear sentence.
2. Give one piece of evidence from the play.
3. Explain in a sentence or two why it supports your position.
Speak slowly enough to be understood and look up from your notes. This is practice for the graded recitation and for presenting ideas in any setting.
✎ CHECK YOURSELF · PICK THE BEST ANSWER
1. How is revising different from proofreading?
2. Why present your thesis out loud?
💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

Revise the argument before you polish the words. Fix the thinking, then the sentences. When you can say your thesis and one reason out loud in plain speech, your essay is ready.
