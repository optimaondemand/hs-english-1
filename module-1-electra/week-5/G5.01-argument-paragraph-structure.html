Grammar Lesson · Week 5
🎯 TODAY'S FOCUS
What is the one order that turns three loose sentences into a paragraph that persuades?
You will build a body paragraph in the order that persuades: a claim sentence, an evidence sentence, and one or more analysis sentences. And you will spot when one of the three is missing.
📚 PART 1 · WARM-UP
💭 READ THE PARAGRAPH · NO WRONG ANSWER
Here is a whole “paragraph”: “Electra is angry. She says her mother is a criminal. This shows the theme.” Something is missing. What has the writer not done?
📚 PART 2 · THE LESSON

A body paragraph in an argument has a fixed shape, and the shape matters as much as the content. It opens with a claim sentence, sometimes called a topic sentence, that states the one point this paragraph will prove and connects it to your thesis. Next comes the evidence sentence, which introduces and quotes a specific line from the play. Then come the analysis sentences, usually two or three, where you explain how the evidence proves the claim. The order is not decoration: a reader who meets the claim first knows what to look for in the evidence, and a reader who meets the analysis last leaves with your thinking, not just Sophocles’. The most common failure is a paragraph that is all claim and evidence with no analysis, a quotation dropped in and left to fend for itself. The second most common is a paragraph with no clear claim, so the evidence floats free of any point. When you draft, you can even label your own sentences to check the shape is complete. If a paragraph has no analysis, it is a report; if it has no claim, it is a note; only all three together make an argument.

ON THE BOARD · ONE PARAGRAPH, THREE JOBS
CLAIM
Sophocles gives Clytaemnestra a real argument, not a villain’s excuse.
EVIDENCE
She insists she killed Agamemnon with “Justice” as her ally, avenging the daughter he sacrificed.
ANALYSIS
By letting her claim the same word Electra claims, Sophocles makes justice contested rather than obvious, so the audience must weigh two duties instead of cheering one side.
The gold labels mark the three jobs. Read top to bottom: claim, then evidence, then analysis.
THE PATTERN, IN YOUR OWN COLORS
CLAIM states the point.   EVIDENCE quotes the proof.   ANALYSIS explains the link.
✎ IDENTIFY & PRACTICE
Label each sentence CLAIM, EVIDENCE, or ANALYSIS.
✎ PICK THE RIGHT LABEL FOR EACH SENTENCE
1. “Chrysothemis is the play’s voice of caution.”
2. “She warns Electra that ‘Justice is sometimes dangerous.’”
3. “This warning complicates the plan, because the sister urging restraint may be the wiser one.”
➡️ NOW WRITE YOUR OWN (IN YOUR ORGANIZER)
Take one body paragraph from your essay draft and label its sentences CLAIM, EVIDENCE, and ANALYSIS. If any label is missing, write the sentence that fills the gap. Remove the labels before you submit: they are a check, not part of the essay.
💪 THE ONE THING TO CARRY FORWARD

Claim, evidence, analysis, in that order. Drop the analysis and you have a report; drop the claim and you have a note. All three together make an argument.
